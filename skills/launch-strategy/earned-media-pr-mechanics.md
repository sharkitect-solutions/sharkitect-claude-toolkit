# Earned Media & PR Mechanics

How media coverage actually works -- from the journalism side, not the marketing side. Most founders treat PR as "send a press release and hope." Effective earned media requires understanding editorial economics.

## Why Journalists Cover Anything

Journalists have three incentives: meet their publishing cadence (1-3 articles/day for online), drive traffic (their career depends on readership metrics), and build their reputation as a domain expert. Your pitch succeeds when it serves ALL THREE simultaneously.

| Journalist Need | What Serves It | What Wastes Their Time |
|---|---|---|
| Publishing cadence | Ready-to-write story with quotes, data, and visuals | Vague pitch requiring extensive follow-up research |
| Traffic metrics | Genuinely novel angle, controversy, or trend piece | "Company launches product" (commodity news) |
| Domain expertise | Exclusive access to data, insight, or trend they can't get elsewhere | Generic press release sent to 500 journalists |

## Pitch Anatomy -- What Actually Gets Opened

Average tech journalist receives 200-400 pitches per week. Open rate on cold PR emails is 3-8% (Muck Rack 2024 State of PR).

| Element | Rule | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Subject line | Under 50 characters. No "Announcing" or "Introducing." Lead with the INSIGHT, not the product | Gmail previews ~50 chars on mobile. "Announcing" signals a press release (auto-delete for most journalists) |
| First sentence | The trend or data point that makes this newsworthy. NOT "I'm reaching out because..." | Journalists decide in 3 seconds. Start with why their readers should care |
| The hook | What makes this story unique RIGHT NOW? Tie to a trend, data point, or contrarian take | "We launched a tool" is not a story. "72% of remote teams waste 5 hours/week on X -- we measured it" is a story |
| The ask | Clear and specific: interview, exclusive, embargo, data access | Vague "let me know if you're interested" gets no response. "Can I send you the data under embargo for a Tuesday piece?" gets action |
| Length | 150 words max for the entire pitch. Attach one-pager with details for those who want more | Long pitches don't get read. The pitch is a trailer, not the movie |
| Personalization | Reference their recent article by name. Explain why YOUR story fits THEIR beat | "I read your piece on [topic] and thought you'd find this relevant because [specific reason]" -- proves you're not mass-emailing |

## Embargo Structure

Embargoes give journalists time to prepare coverage before public announcement, creating a coordinated wave of articles on launch day.

| Embargo Type | How It Works | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| Hard embargo | Journalist agrees in writing not to publish before a specific date/time | Major launches with multiple press targets. Sends press kit 5-7 business days before launch |
| Soft embargo | Informal agreement, usually verbal, "we'd love for this to go live Tuesday" | Smaller launches, trusted journalist relationships. Less control but lower friction |
| Exclusive | One journalist gets the story first. Others can cover after the exclusive publishes | When you need ONE high-quality piece (TechCrunch, The Verge) more than breadth. Trade exclusivity for depth of coverage |
| No embargo | Information is public immediately when shared | Feature updates, minor news. Most launches don't need embargoes |

**Embargo rules**:
- NEVER break your own embargo (posting on social media before the embargo lifts). Journalists will blacklist you permanently
- If a journalist breaks embargo accidentally, notify all other embargoed journalists immediately and release them from the embargo
- Embargo is a trust relationship. First-time pitchers rarely get hard embargoes from top-tier outlets. Build the relationship with smaller stories first
- Include embargo terms explicitly in writing: "This information is under embargo until [date] at [time] [timezone]. By opening the attached materials, you agree to these terms"

## Editorial Calendar Timing

Publications operate on predictable cycles. Timing your pitch to these cycles dramatically increases coverage probability.

| Publication Type | Planning Horizon | Best Pitch Window | Worst Time |
|---|---|---|---|
| Major tech blogs (TechCrunch, The Verge) | 1-2 weeks | Monday-Wednesday morning. Pitch by 10 AM ET | Friday afternoon, holiday weeks |
| Industry trades (SaaStr, First Round Review) | 2-4 weeks | 3-4 weeks before desired publication date | Day before a major industry conference (all bandwidth consumed) |
| Newsletters (Lenny's, TLDR, Morning Brew) | 1-2 weeks for featured, 3-5 days for mention | Tuesday-Wednesday for following week inclusion | Monday (issue already set) |
| Podcasts | 4-8 weeks | Pitch 6-8 weeks before desired air date; recording scheduled 2-4 weeks before air | Less than 2 weeks out (schedule is locked) |
| Print magazines | 3-6 months | Pitch 4-6 months before publication date | Less than 2 months out |

**Seasonal patterns**: January (predictions/trends), March-April (spring launches, pre-conference), September (fall launches, back-to-business), avoid late November-December (holiday content dominates, journalists on vacation).

## Press Kit Optimization

What journalists actually use from press kits (based on PR industry surveys):

| Asset | Usage Rate | Format | Common Mistake |
|---|---|---|---|
| High-res product screenshots | 85% | PNG, min 2000px wide, light AND dark backgrounds | Providing only marketing mockups instead of real product screenshots |
| Founder headshot | 70% | 1MB+ JPEG, professional quality, plain background | Selfie or team photo instead of individual headshot |
| One-page fact sheet | 65% | PDF, single page with key stats, founding date, funding, user count | Multi-page company overview no one reads past page 1 |
| Prepared quotes | 60% | 2-3 quotes attributed to specific people, ready to copy-paste | Generic quotes that could apply to any company |
| Product demo video | 45% | Under 90 seconds, no narration (journalist may voice over), hosted URL | 5-minute marketing video with music and transitions |
| Logo files | 40% | SVG + PNG, light and dark versions, with clear space guidelines | Only providing a JPEG ripped from the website |
| Comparison data | 35% | Table or chart showing measurable difference vs alternatives | Subjective claims without data ("10x better") |

**The "ready-to-write" test**: A journalist should be able to write a complete 500-word article using ONLY your press kit, without a single follow-up question. If they can't, your kit is incomplete.

## Warm Introduction Economics

Cold pitches: 3-8% response rate. Warm introductions: 40-60% response rate (Cision 2024).

| Intro Source | Effectiveness | How to Get It |
|---|---|---|
| Mutual portfolio company (if VC-backed) | Highest -- journalist trusts the source | Ask your investors who in their portfolio has been covered by target journalist |
| Industry peer who's been covered | High -- journalist remembers the relationship | Build founder network relationships BEFORE you need press. Offer to be a source for their stories first |
| PR firm with existing relationships | Medium-High -- depends on the firm's track record with that journalist | Expensive ($5K-15K/month) but guarantees access. Only worth it for major launches |
| Twitter/LinkedIn engagement over time | Medium -- builds familiarity but not obligation | Comment thoughtfully on journalist's articles for 3-6 months before pitching. Become a recognized name in their mentions |
| Cold pitch with exceptional hook | Low but non-zero -- the story has to be genuinely compelling | Reserve for truly newsworthy moments. One exceptional cold pitch is better than ten mediocre warm intros |

## Measuring Earned Media Impact

**AVE (Advertising Value Equivalency) is dead**. It was the old method of estimating "how much would this coverage cost as an ad?" but no serious PR professional uses it anymore -- it compares apples to oranges.

| Metric | What It Measures | How to Track |
|---|---|---|
| Referral traffic | Direct visits from media coverage | UTM parameters on all links in press materials. Google Analytics referral source |
| Share of voice | Your mentions vs competitor mentions in target publications | Media monitoring tools (Meltwater, Mention, Google Alerts for budget) |
| Message pull-through | Whether journalists used YOUR framing and key messages | Manual review: did the article use your positioning, or reframe the story? |
| Conversion from coverage | Signups/trials that originated from press coverage | UTM tracking + "where did you hear about us?" survey on signup form |
| Domain authority impact | Backlinks from high-authority publications | Ahrefs, Moz -- press backlinks from DA 70+ sites compound SEO value for months |
| Social amplification | How widely the article was shared beyond initial publication | Track social shares of the article URL (BuzzSumo, social monitoring) |

**The real metric**: What percentage of press coverage mentioned your product in the context YOU wanted? If 80% of articles used your positioning frame, PR succeeded. If most articles reframed the story, your messaging failed regardless of volume.
