# Campaign Templates Reference

## Cold Email Templates by Offer Type

### Template 1: Diagnostic / Free Consultation Offer

**Subject:** quick question
**Body:**
```
Hi [First Name],

Most [industry] companies I talk to are losing 15-30% of their [key metric] to problems they can't see from the inside.

I run a free [length] diagnostic that maps exactly where those leaks are -- no pitch, just a clear picture of what's happening.

Would it be worth 15 minutes this week to see if anything's hiding?

[Signature]
```

**Why it works:** Doesn't claim to know their problems. Creates curiosity about invisible losses. Low-commitment CTA (15 minutes, not a demo or sales call).

### Template 2: Demo / Trial Offer

**Subject:** [company name] + [your product category]
**Body:**
```
Hi [First Name],

[Company] looks like you're doing [specific thing they do well] -- which usually means [related pain point] is eating more time than it should.

We built [Product] specifically for [their segment]. [One-sentence outcome claim with number].

Want to see how it works in a 15-minute walkthrough?

[Signature]
```

**Why it works:** Opens with genuine observation (not flattery). Connects their strength to a related problem. Specific outcome claim. Direct CTA.

### Template 3: Content / Event Invite

**Subject:** [topic] for [role]s
**Body:**
```
Hi [First Name],

We're running a [format] on [topic] -- specifically how [relevant outcome] without [common objection].

[One sentence on who else is attending or what's covered].

Worth a look? [Link]

[Signature]
```

**Why it works:** Ultra-short. Specific audience targeting in subject. Social proof or content preview. Single link, no pressure.

### Template 4: Direct Service Offer

**Subject:** [their pain point]
**Body:**
```
Hi [First Name],

Quick question -- how are you currently handling [specific operational challenge]?

We help [segment] companies [specific outcome] by [brief method]. [Company similar to theirs] saw [specific result] in [timeframe].

If that's relevant, I'd love to share how. If not, no worries at all.

[Signature]
```

**Why it works:** Opens with a question (invites reply). Specific proof point. Permission-based close removes pressure.

## Follow-Up Sequence Frameworks

### 3-Touch Sequence (Micro-Campaigns, <50 Contacts)

| Touch | Day | Purpose | Angle |
|---|---|---|---|
| Initial | 0 | Problem awareness + offer | Core value proposition |
| FU1 | 3-4 | New proof or data | Case study, statistic, or industry trend |
| FU2 | 7-10 | Soft close / breakup | Low-friction reminder + graceful exit |

**Best for:** High personalization campaigns (Level 3-4), expensive-to-reach prospects, enterprise targets.

### 5-Touch Sequence (Standard Campaigns, 50-200 Contacts)

| Touch | Day | Purpose | Angle |
|---|---|---|---|
| Initial | 0 | Problem awareness + offer | Core value proposition |
| FU1 | 3 | Education | Invisible problem they haven't considered |
| FU2 | 7 | Social proof | Case study or result from similar company |
| FU3 | 14 | Different angle | Time/cost framing, seasonal urgency, or competitive pressure |
| FU4 | 21 | Breakup | Graceful exit, leave door open |

**Best for:** Most campaigns. Balances persistence with respect for inbox.

### 7-Touch Sequence (High-Volume, 200+ Contacts)

| Touch | Day | Purpose | Angle |
|---|---|---|---|
| Initial | 0 | Problem awareness + offer | Core value proposition |
| FU1 | 2 | Quick bump | Ultra-short, reference the value not the email |
| FU2 | 5 | Education | Industry-specific insight or data |
| FU3 | 9 | Social proof | Case study with specific numbers |
| FU4 | 14 | Different problem | Adjacent pain point they may care about more |
| FU5 | 21 | Re-engagement | New offer or updated value prop |
| FU6 | 30 | Breakup | Graceful exit |

**Best for:** Lower ACV deals, SMB campaigns, industries with high email volume tolerance. Use with caution -- 7 touches from an unfamiliar sender tests patience.

### Follow-Up Copy Rules

1. **Each follow-up stands alone.** A recipient who only sees FU3 should understand the value without reading the initial.
2. **Never reference previous emails.** No "just following up," "per my last email," "circling back," "bumping this to the top."
3. **Each touch introduces something new:** different proof point, different angle, different framing of the same problem.
4. **Follow-ups get shorter, not longer.** Initial: 60-120 words. FU1-FU3: 40-80 words. Breakup: 20-40 words.
5. **Breakup email is the highest-converting follow-up** in most sequences (10-15% of total replies come from the breakup). Keep it genuine and brief.
6. **Stop all sends immediately when a reply is received** -- positive, negative, or neutral. Never send a follow-up after a reply.

### Breakup Email Template

**Subject:** (same thread)
**Body:**
```
Hi [First Name],

Looks like the timing isn't right -- totally understand.

If [core problem] becomes a priority down the road, happy to pick this back up.

All the best,
[Name]
```

## Subject Line Patterns

### High-Performance Patterns (Based on Aggregate Cold Email Data)

| Pattern | Example | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|
| Internal memo style | "quick question" | Looks like it's from a colleague, bypasses promotional filters |
| Name + topic | "Sarah - scheduling" | Personal + relevant, high open rate in SMB |
| Lowercase, casual | "thought about this" | Signals human, not automation |
| Specific + short | "Q3 pipeline" | Implies insider knowledge of their priorities |
| Question format | "handling [pain point]?" | Invites mental engagement before opening |

### Patterns to Avoid

| Pattern | Example | Why It Fails |
|---|---|---|
| Hype language | "Exclusive opportunity!" | Spam filter trigger, trained to ignore |
| Long subjects | "How [Company] Can Save 30% on Operations" | Truncated on mobile, looks like marketing |
| ALL CAPS anything | "IMPORTANT" | Spam signal, feels aggressive |
| Emoji in subject | "Hey Sarah! :wave:" | Spam filter trigger in cold email context |
| Clickbait | "You won't believe..." | Destroys trust before the email is opened |

### A/B Testing Protocol
- Test 2 subject lines per campaign (50/50 split)
- Minimum sample: 50 emails per variant before drawing conclusions
- Measure open rate only (subject line doesn't affect reply rate directly)
- Run for full sequence completion before declaring winner
- Only change one variable at a time: subject line OR body, never both

## Personalization Frameworks by Depth Level

### Level 1: Basic (Automated, No Research)
- First name, company name, industry
- **Use when:** High-volume (500+), low ACV (<$500), SMB
- **Expected lift:** Baseline (no lift over unpersonalized)

### Level 2: Industry-Specific (Semi-Automated)
- Level 1 + industry-specific pain points, seasonal context, role-appropriate language
- **How:** Build 3-5 industry-specific email variants. Assign by industry tag.
- **Use when:** Scaled campaigns (100-500), moderate ACV ($500-5K)
- **Expected lift:** 1.5-2x over Level 1

### Level 3: Role-Specific (Manual + Template)
- Level 2 + title-aware framing, company-size context, tech stack references
- **How:** Research role responsibilities for their company size. Adjust framing.
- **Use when:** Micro-campaigns (<100), higher ACV ($5K-25K)
- **Expected lift:** 2.5-3.5x over Level 1

### Level 4: Account-Specific (Fully Manual)
- Level 3 + company-specific research, recent news/funding/hires, mutual connections, specific product/feature references
- **How:** 5-10 minutes of research per prospect. Custom opening line minimum.
- **Use when:** Enterprise campaigns (<25), high ACV ($25K+)
- **Expected lift:** 4-6x over Level 1

## Niche Rotation Strategy

When running campaigns across multiple industries simultaneously:

### Why Rotate (Not Blast)
- Sending to one industry cluster simultaneously creates detectable patterns for spam filters
- Rotation allows industry-specific copy optimization per send window
- Spreading across industries means one bad campaign doesn't contaminate all reputation
- Different industries have different peak engagement times

### Rotation Schedule Example (5 Niches, 5 Days)

| Day | Primary Niche | Secondary Niche |
|---|---|---|
| Monday | Industry A | Industry B |
| Tuesday | Industry C | -- |
| Wednesday | Industry D | Industry E |
| Thursday | Industry A | Industry C |
| Friday | Industry B | Industry D |

**Rules:**
- Never send to the same niche on consecutive days
- Pair high-volume niches with low-volume ones to balance daily send count
- Reserve one day for catch-up, re-sends, or new niche testing
- Align with industry patterns when known (e.g., real estate on Friday for weekend prep)
