# Email Timing and Follow-Up Strategy

Load when timing matters, planning follow-ups, managing cross-timezone communication, or deciding whether email is the right medium for the message.

## Optimal Send Times

Timing affects open rates by 20-40%. These are research-backed patterns, not rules -- always adjust for the specific recipient when you know their habits.

| Recipient Type | Best Send Window | Worst Send Window | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| C-suite / executives | Tuesday-Thursday, 7-8 AM their timezone | Friday afternoon, weekends | Execs process inbox early morning. Friday PM is mentally checked out. |
| Engineers / technical | Tuesday-Thursday, 10-11 AM their timezone | Monday morning (backlog), Friday PM | Engineers batch-process email mid-morning after standup and deep work block. |
| Sales / BD | Tuesday-Wednesday, 8-9 AM their timezone | Monday (pipeline review), Thursday-Friday (closing) | Sales are most responsive early-week before pipeline pressure builds. |
| External clients | Tuesday-Thursday, 9-10 AM their timezone | Monday AM (their own backlog), any time outside business hours | Clients who receive emails at midnight question your work-life balance and project management. |
| Vendors | Monday-Wednesday, any business hour | Friday PM (they'll lose it over the weekend) | Vendors are most responsive early-week when managing their own workloads. |
| Cross-functional teams | Tuesday-Wednesday, 10 AM the timezone of the majority | Anything that arrives outside business hours for ANY member | Global teams require timezone awareness. One person's 10 AM is another's midnight. |

**"Schedule Send" rule**: If you write an email at 11 PM, schedule it for 8 AM tomorrow. Sending late at night creates implicit pressure to respond late, sets bad cultural norms, and signals poor time management -- not dedication.

## Follow-Up Timing and Cadence

| Urgency | First Follow-Up | Second Follow-Up | After 2 Follow-Ups |
|---|---|---|---|
| Time-sensitive (deadline within 48h) | Same day, 4+ hours later | Next morning | Escalate or call. Email has failed. |
| Standard business request | 3 business days | 5 business days after first follow-up | Change approach: try different channel, different person, or re-evaluate the ask. |
| Low priority / FYI that needs acknowledgment | 5 business days | 10 business days | Accept that it's not a priority for them. Close the loop: "I'll proceed with [default action] unless I hear otherwise." |
| External client | 2 business days (clients expect responsiveness) | 4 business days | Escalate within your org: involve account manager or CSM. |
| Job application / networking | 5-7 business days | 14 business days (one follow-up only) | Stop. Two follow-ups is the professional limit for outbound networking. |

**Follow-up content rule**: Every follow-up must add something new. New information, new angle, new deadline, new context. If you have nothing new to add, you don't have a follow-up -- you have a reminder, and reminders are annoying.

### Follow-Up Subject Lines

| Approach | When to Use | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Same thread (Reply) | Follow-up is directly related, keeping context together | Reply to original email |
| New subject with reference | Original thread is 5+ deep, or topic has evolved | "Updated: Q3 vendor decision (following up from March thread)" |
| Fresh email, no reference | It's been 2+ weeks with no response. Old thread is buried. | New subject, brief recap of context in body |

## Timezone Management

| Scenario | Best Practice |
|---|---|
| You know their timezone | Always send during their business hours. Schedule send if needed. |
| You don't know their timezone | Check their LinkedIn location, email signature, or company HQ. When in doubt, 10 AM ET/PT is safest for US contacts. |
| Global team (3+ timezones) | Send during the overlap window. If no overlap exists, rotate send times across the team so the same people aren't always receiving after-hours. |
| Scheduling across timezones | Always include timezone in proposed times: "3 PM ET / 12 PM PT / 8 PM GMT." Never write just "3 PM." |
| Urgent cross-timezone | If it's 2 AM in their timezone, use a different channel (Slack, text). Urgent email that arrives while they're asleep is not urgent -- it's tomorrow's email. |

**Timezone trap**: Scheduling a meeting at "the most convenient time for me" that falls at 7 AM or 7 PM for someone else. Distribute timezone inconvenience equitably across recurring meetings.

## Email vs Other Medium Decision

Not every message should be an email. Using the wrong medium for the message type causes friction.

| Message Type | Best Medium | When Email Is Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| Quick question (<1 min to answer) | Slack/Teams/chat | Email forces open -> read -> compose -> send. Chat takes 10 seconds. |
| Status update (no action needed) | Email or async update tool (Loom, Notion) | Chat buries updates. Email creates a record. |
| Complex decision with nuance | Call or video, then email summary | Back-and-forth emails take 3 days; a 15-min call resolves it in 15 minutes. |
| Emotional or sensitive content | Call or in-person, then email to document | Text strips 93% of emotional communication (Mehrabian). Don't deliver bad news in monotone. |
| Time-sensitive (need answer in <2 hours) | Chat or phone | Email open rates are 20-30%. If you need a response NOW, email is unreliable. |
| Formal agreement or decision record | Email | Chat messages are ephemeral. Email creates searchable, permanent record. |
| Introduction to someone new | Email | Chat introductions feel informal and don't let the recipient respond on their own time. |
| Detailed proposal or document sharing | Email with attachment | Chat file sharing is unreliable (files expire, buried in threads). |

**Medium escalation ladder**: Chat (quick, informal) -> Email (structured, recorded) -> Call (nuanced, emotional) -> Video (full context) -> In-person (highest bandwidth). Escalate the medium when the message is misunderstood or the stakes increase.

## Email Chain Management

Long email chains become unreadable. Managing them is part of writing effective email.

| Situation | Action |
|---|---|
| Chain > 5 replies deep | Start a new email summarizing the thread: "To summarize where we are: [bullets]. The open question is: [one thing]." |
| Topic shifted mid-chain | Start a new email with a new subject line. "Splitting this from the vendor thread -- the pricing question deserves its own discussion." |
| New person added mid-chain | Provide inline context: "[Name], adding you for context on the API discussion. Summary: [2-3 bullets]. Your input needed on: [specific ask]." |
| Reply-all vs Reply | Reply-all: when your response affects everyone on the thread. Reply: when it only matters to the sender. When in doubt, reply to sender only. |
| Forward with commentary | Your commentary goes ABOVE the forwarded message, not inline. Start with "Forwarding for your awareness" or "Forwarding because I need your input on [specific thing]." |
| Dead thread revival | If replying to a thread older than 2 weeks, add context: "Revisiting this -- the situation has changed because [reason]." Don't assume they remember. |

## Read Receipts and Tracking

| Tool | What It Does | Ethics |
|---|---|---|
| Read receipts (Outlook/Gmail) | Notifies you when recipient opens the email | Acceptable in enterprise where it's a standard feature. Feels surveillance-like in external communication. |
| Pixel tracking (Mailtrack, Superhuman) | Invisible pixel loads when email is opened, logging time and location | Ethically questionable without disclosure. Some recipients use email clients that block tracking. Unreliable signal. |
| Link tracking | Click-tracked URLs reveal when someone clicks your attachment or link | Acceptable for sales and marketing. Uncomfortable for personal or peer communication. |

**Tracking rule**: If you would be uncomfortable telling the recipient you're tracking their email opens, you should not be tracking their email opens. Professional trust is worth more than open-rate data.

## Inbox Management for Recipients

Writing emails that are easy for the recipient to process increases response rates.

| Principle | Implementation |
|---|---|
| Respect their inbox triage | Most people scan subject lines and first 2 lines. Put the critical info there. |
| One email = one topic | If you have 2 unrelated asks, send 2 emails. Each can be handled, forwarded, or filed independently. |
| Make it reply-friendly | If you need a simple answer, structure the email so they can reply "yes" or "Option B." Don't force them to write a paragraph. |
| Archive-friendly subject | Subjects should be searchable in 6 months: "Q3 Vendor Decision: Acme vs Beta" not "Quick question." |
| Reduce back-and-forth | Anticipate their follow-up questions and answer them preemptively. "The cost is $5K (within our quarterly budget of $20K). Timeline is 3 weeks (I've cleared this with engineering)." |
