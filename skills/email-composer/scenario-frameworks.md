# Email Scenario Frameworks

Load when composing an email for a specific scenario that needs structural guidance. These are structural frameworks, not fill-in-the-blank templates. Adapt structure to the actual relationship and context.

## Meeting Request Framework

| Element | Content | Order |
|---|---|---|
| Purpose | Why this meeting matters to THEM, not you | First sentence |
| Duration | Specific length ("30 min" not "quick chat") | Second sentence |
| Proposed times | 2-3 specific options with timezone | Third element |
| Agenda preview | 1-3 bullet points of what you'll cover | After times |
| Easy out | "If email works better, happy to handle async" | Final line before sign-off |

**Common failure**: Meeting requests that explain your entire situation before revealing the ask. Recipient decides in 5 seconds whether to accept -- lead with value to them.

**Scheduling trap**: "Let me know when you're free" forces them to do the scheduling work. Propose 3 times, include a Calendly/Cal.com link as backup, and name who will send the invite.

## Follow-Up Framework

| Follow-Up Type | Timing | Structure | Tone |
|---|---|---|---|
| After no response to request | 3 business days (2 for urgent) | New value or context + restated ask + deadline | "Bringing this back to the top of your inbox" -- not "just checking in" |
| After a meeting | Same day or next morning | Thank + key decisions + action items with owners + next meeting | Quick, factual, establishes written record |
| After they completed your ask | Within 24 hours | Specific acknowledgment of what they did + impact it had | Genuine, brief, closes the loop |
| After introduction | Within 4 hours of intro email | Reply-all to intro, then move introducer to BCC on next reply | Fast response signals you value the connection |
| After proposal/pitch | 5-7 business days | New proof point or case study + soft ask | Add value, don't just ask "have you decided?" |

**Follow-up anti-pattern**: "Just following up" / "Just checking in" / "Circling back" -- these phrases signal you have nothing new to add. Every follow-up must contain new information, a new angle, or a clearer deadline.

**Follow-up escalation**: After 2 follow-ups with no response, one of three things is true: (1) they're not the right person, (2) the ask isn't compelling enough, (3) the timing is wrong. Sending a 3rd identical follow-up won't fix any of these. Change approach.

## Introduction Email Framework

| Type | Structure | Key Rules |
|---|---|---|
| Requesting an intro | Who you want to meet + why + what's in it for THEM + permission to opt out | Never ask someone to introduce you without explaining why the other person would benefit |
| Making an intro | One paragraph on Person A (relevant to B) + one on Person B (relevant to A) + why they should talk | Both parties must have opted in. Blind intros burn social capital. |
| Receiving an intro | Reply-all: thank introducer + express interest to the other person. Next email: move introducer to BCC | The "move to BCC" is a social norm. Not doing it floods the introducer with your subsequent thread. |

**Double opt-in rule**: Before introducing two people, confirm with BOTH that they want the introduction. Single opt-in intros ("I gave your email to someone") feel invasive and put the recipient in an awkward position.

## Feedback Request Framework

| Element | Content | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Context | What you need feedback on + why their perspective specifically | "I'd love anyone's thoughts" = nobody's responsibility |
| Scope | Exactly what kind of feedback you want | "What do you think?" is too open. "Is the pricing section clear?" is actionable. |
| Constraints | What is NOT changeable (decisions already made) | Prevents feedback on things you can't act on, which frustrates both parties |
| Format | How to deliver feedback (inline comments, reply email, call) | Reduce friction -- some people hate writing long emails |
| Deadline | When you need it by | "Whenever you get a chance" = never |

**Feedback scope trap**: Asking for "any feedback" on a 40-page document. The reader doesn't know if you want typo catches or strategic direction. Narrow the scope: "Please focus on Section 3 -- does the pricing model make sense for enterprise customers?"

## Status Update Framework

| Element | Content | Order |
|---|---|---|
| Bottom line | Where things stand in one sentence (on track / at risk / blocked) | First |
| Key metrics or milestones | 2-4 bullet points with numbers | Second |
| What changed since last update | Only deltas, not a full recap | Third |
| Blockers or risks | Named, with owner and proposed resolution | Fourth |
| Next steps | What happens next + who + when | Last |

**Status update anti-pattern**: The "everything is fine" update that buries a risk in paragraph 4. If there is a risk, the first sentence of the update must name it. Surprises destroy trust faster than bad news.

## Decline / Say-No Framework

| Element | Content | Tone |
|---|---|---|
| Gratitude or acknowledgment | Thank them for asking / acknowledge the opportunity | Genuine, 1 sentence max |
| Clear no | State the decline without hedging | "I won't be able to" not "I'm not sure I can" |
| Brief reason (optional) | 1 sentence max. Not required. | Avoid over-explaining -- it invites negotiation |
| Alternative (if genuine) | Suggest someone else, a different timeline, or a different scope | Only if you mean it. Fake alternatives are worse than no alternative. |

**Decline traps**:
- "Let me think about it" when you mean no -- delays the inevitable and wastes their time
- "I'm so sorry, I really wish I could but..." -- excessive apology signals guilt, not professionalism
- Ghosting (no response) -- always worse than a clear no. Even "I can't, sorry" is better than silence.

## Decision Request Framework

| Element | Content | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Decision statement | What decision is needed, in one sentence | "We need to choose between Vendor A and Vendor B for Q3" |
| Options (2-3 max) | Each option with 1-line pros and 1-line cons | More than 3 options paralyzes. Pre-filter to the viable ones. |
| Recommendation | Your recommendation with brief rationale | Decision-makers want a recommendation to react to, not an open question |
| Deadline | When the decision is needed and why | "We need to notify the vendor by Friday to lock pricing" |
| Default if no response | What happens if they don't respond | "If I don't hear back by Friday, I'll proceed with Option A" -- only for peer/manager emails, never for C-suite |

**Decision request failure**: Presenting 5+ options with a full analysis for each. The recipient is not doing your job. Filter to 2-3, recommend one, and make it easy to say "go with your recommendation."

## Apology / Mistake Acknowledgment Framework

| Element | Content | Critical Rule |
|---|---|---|
| Acknowledgment | Name what went wrong, specifically | Never say "mistakes were made" (passive). Say "I missed the deadline." |
| Impact | State the impact on THEM | Shows you understand why it matters, not just that it happened |
| Cause (brief) | 1 sentence on why | Not an excuse. "I underestimated the complexity" not "I was really busy" |
| Fix | What you have already done or will do | Past tense preferred: "I've already..." shows action, not just intention |
| Prevention | How this won't happen again | Systemic fix, not "I'll try harder" |

**Apology trap**: Over-apologizing across multiple emails. One clear, comprehensive apology is professional. Repeated apologies become about your guilt, not their problem. Apologize once, then demonstrate the fix through action.

## Vendor Negotiation Framework

| Element | Content | Tone |
|---|---|---|
| Appreciation | Acknowledge their proposal/offer | Professional, not warm |
| Counter/ask | State your counter-position with rationale | "Based on our volume, we'd expect..." not "Can you do better?" |
| Leverage (subtle) | Reference alternatives, timeline pressure, or budget constraints | Never bluff about alternatives you don't have |
| Next step | Propose a specific next action | "Can we schedule a call Thursday to discuss?" |

**Negotiation via email rule**: Email is best for establishing positions and documenting agreements. The actual negotiation (give-and-take) should happen on a call. Email back-and-forth creates escalating position statements, not compromise.
