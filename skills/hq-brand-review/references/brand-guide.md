# Sharkitect Digital Brand Voice Guide

## Brand Identity

**Three-layer brand model** (canonical SoT: HQ K1 `brand-identity-guide.md` §1.5; locked 2026-05-01).

- **Category** (industry / market layer): **AI Transformation Agency**
- **Offering** (engagement model layer): **AI Transformation Partnership**
- **Outcome** (client transformation layer): **systems-first AI-driven business**

These three layers are distinct and must NOT be collapsed. "Agency" names the category we operate in; "Partnership" names the way we engage clients; "systems-first AI-driven business" names what the client becomes after the engagement. When reviewing content, flag conflation (e.g., "AI Transformation Partner" used as a category descriptor is incorrect — that's offering language standing in for category language).

**Who we are**: An AI Transformation Agency that delivers AI Transformation Partnerships to SMBs, turning overwhelmed business owners into operators with leverage running systems-first AI-driven businesses.

**Who we serve**: Small-to-medium businesses (5-50 employees) in trades, services, and professional services. They're good at their craft but drowning in operations.

**Our position**: The technical co-founder they never had. Not a vendor — a partner who builds systems, not just deliverables. Partnership-but-authoritative posture (we run the engagement, the client is the operator who has to live with the systems we leave behind).

## Voice Attributes (Detailed)

### 1. Confident (Target: 7-9/10)

**What it sounds like:**
- "Here's what we'll build." (not "We could potentially explore building...")
- "This solves your scheduling problem." (not "This might help with scheduling.")
- Active voice. Present tense. Declarative sentences.

**Linguistic markers (positive):**
- Active voice constructions
- Present tense for capabilities ("We build" not "We can build")
- Concrete specifics over abstract descriptions
- Numbers and outcomes over adjectives

**Linguistic markers (negative — ban these):**
- "We believe that..." (weakens authority)
- "It's worth noting that..." (hedge phrase)
- "Might", "could", "possibly", "potentially" (uncertainty cluster)
- "We think" before a recommendation (if you don't know, don't recommend)

### 2. Direct (Target: 7-9/10)

**What it sounds like:**
- Leading with the point, not the context
- One idea per paragraph
- Short sentences for impact. Longer sentences only for complex explanations.
- "No" is a complete sentence when something doesn't fit

**Linguistic markers (positive):**
- First sentence of each paragraph = the point
- Average sentence length: 12-18 words
- Paragraph length: 2-4 sentences
- Contractions used naturally (we'll, don't, isn't)

**Linguistic markers (negative — ban these):**
- "In order to" (just say "to")
- "It's important to understand that" (just state the thing)
- "As we discussed previously" (just make the point)
- "Moving forward" (filler — delete it, the sentence still works)
- Paragraphs over 5 sentences

### 3. Expert (Target: 6-8/10)

**What it sounds like:**
- Using precise terms when they matter, plain language when they don't
- Explaining complex concepts through concrete examples, not abstractions
- Citing specific outcomes and numbers, not vague claims
- Acknowledging limitations honestly (experts know what they don't know)

**Linguistic markers (positive):**
- Technical terms explained on first use when audience is non-technical
- Specific numbers ("reduced response time from 4 hours to 15 minutes")
- Honest constraints ("this works for teams under 50; beyond that, the architecture changes")

**Linguistic markers (negative — ban these):**
- "Best-in-class", "world-class", "cutting-edge" (empty superlatives)
- "Leverage" as a verb, "synergy", "paradigm shift" (corporate buzzwords)
- "AI-powered" without explaining what the AI actually does
- Jargon without explanation when writing for business owners

### 4. Approachable (Target: 6-8/10)

**What it sounds like:**
- Talking like a smart friend who happens to be technical
- Using "you" and "your" more than "we" and "our"
- Acknowledging the reader's experience and challenges
- Occasional humor that's dry, not forced

**Linguistic markers (positive):**
- Second person ("you'll see results" not "clients see results")
- Conversational transitions (natural flow, not academic connectors)
- Contractions (don't, we'll, it's) — we're people, not press releases
- Questions as engagement (but not rhetorical questions in headers)

**Linguistic markers (negative — ban these):**
- "Dear valued client" (we're not a bank)
- Formal salutations in emails beyond first contact
- "Please do not hesitate to reach out" (just say "let us know")
- Exclamation marks more than once per piece (forced enthusiasm)

### 5. Action-Oriented (Target: 7-9/10)

**What it sounds like:**
- Every communication ends with a clear next step
- Proposals include specific timelines and deliverables
- Recommendations are actionable, not theoretical
- "Here's what to do" over "here are some considerations"

**Linguistic markers (positive):**
- Imperative mood for recommendations ("Schedule the call" not "You might want to consider scheduling")
- Specific dates and deadlines
- Numbered action items
- Clear ownership ("I'll handle X; you'll need to do Y")

**Linguistic markers (negative — ban these):**
- "Consider doing X" without a recommendation
- "Various options are available" without listing them
- Conclusions that summarize without recommending
- "Let me know your thoughts" as a closer (weak — propose a specific next step)

## Banned Terms & Phrases

### Absolute Bans (never use)

| Term | Reason | Use Instead |
|------|--------|-------------|
| "Leverage" (as verb) | Corporate buzzword | "Use", "apply", "build on" |
| "Synergy" / "synergize" | Empty jargon | Describe the specific benefit |
| "Best-in-class" | Unverifiable superlative | Cite specific metrics |
| "Paradigm shift" | Overused to meaninglessness | Describe what actually changed |
| "Please do not hesitate" | Formal filler | "Let us know" or "reach out anytime" |
| "At the end of the day" | Cliche | Delete — sentence works without it |
| "It goes without saying" | Then don't say it | Delete the phrase entirely |
| "Think outside the box" | Cliche | Describe the specific creative approach |
| "Low-hanging fruit" | Cliche | "Quick wins" or describe specifically |

### Contextual Bans (avoid unless technically accurate)

| Term | When Banned | When Allowed |
|------|------------|--------------|
| "AI-powered" | Marketing without specifics | Technical docs explaining the AI component |
| "Scalable" | Vague claim | Specific context ("scales to 10K concurrent") |
| "Robust" | Vague adjective | Technical context with specific meaning |
| "Seamless" | Marketing hyperbole | Never — nothing is actually seamless |
| "Revolutionary" | Almost always hyperbole | Never — let the work speak |

## Linguistic & Psychological Foundations

These cross-domain principles explain WHY the brand voice rules work. Use them to resolve ambiguous cases where the rules above do not give a clear answer.

### Sociolinguistic Register Theory (Halliday's Field/Tenor/Mode)

Every communication operates on three dimensions that determine appropriate language:
- **Field** (subject matter): Business operations, digital transformation, AI systems. Our Field demands precision without obscurity -- we name specific tools, timelines, and outcomes.
- **Tenor** (relationship between participants): Expert peer relationship. NOT teacher-student, NOT vendor-client, NOT friend-friend. We are the technical co-founder they never had. This means we explain without condescending and recommend without hedging.
- **Mode** (channel of communication): Written digital. Written mode demands tighter structure than spoken. Digital mode demands scannability -- front-loaded points, short paragraphs, visual hierarchy.

When voice attribute scores conflict, resolve by asking: Does this match our Field (business ops), Tenor (expert peer), and Mode (written digital)? If yes, the content is on-brand regardless of individual attribute wobble.

### Cognitive Processing Fluency (Reber, Schwarz & Bornstein)

Text that is easy to process feels more trustworthy, more credible, and more true. This is not opinion -- it is replicated experimental psychology. Processing fluency directly impacts whether a reader trusts what we say.

Practical implications for brand voice:
- **Sentence length 12-18 words optimal.** Beyond 25 words, processing fluency drops sharply. Comprehension falls 10% per 5 additional words past the 20-word threshold.
- **One idea per sentence.** Compound ideas force re-reading, which signals difficulty, which reduces trust.
- **Familiar word forms over formal synonyms.** "Use" not "utilize". "Help" not "facilitate". The simpler word is processed 15-40ms faster, and that speed registers as trustworthiness.
- **Consistent formatting across pieces.** Readers who encounter our content in proposals, emails, and blog posts build a processing template. Breaking format forces re-calibration and resets fluency gains.

### Mere Exposure Effect (Zajonc, 1968)

People develop preference for things they encounter repeatedly -- including brand voice patterns. Each piece of Sharkitect content that sounds consistent reinforces familiarity. Familiarity creates preference. Preference creates trust.

The inverse is equally powerful: each voice deviation resets the familiarity counter for that reader. A single off-brand email after 10 on-brand touches does not average out. It creates cognitive dissonance ("this does not sound like them") that actively erodes built trust. This is why brand review exists -- not to enforce rules for their own sake, but because inconsistency has a measurable psychological cost.

## AI Content Guidelines

All AI-generated content must pass brand review because AI defaults to these anti-brand patterns:

1. **Over-qualification**: AI adds "might", "could", "potentially" to hedge. We don't hedge.
2. **Symmetric structures**: AI loves parallel sentence patterns. Natural writing varies rhythm.
3. **Filler transitions**: "Furthermore", "Moreover", "In addition" — delete these. The next sentence should stand alone.
4. **Generic closings**: "I hope this helps!" — never. End with a specific next step.
5. **Enthusiasm inflation**: "That's a great question!" — never. Just answer the question.
