# Quantified Effects Reference

Load when the user asks "how much impact does X have?", wants to justify a psychological approach with data, needs to prioritize which principles to apply based on expected effect size, or is building a business case for a CRO change. Do NOT load for general principle selection or when the user already knows which approach to use.

## Effect Sizes by Principle

These are published research findings. Effect sizes vary by context -- use as directional estimates, not guarantees.

### Pricing Psychology

| Effect | Key Finding | Source | Marketing Application | Boundary Condition |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Anchoring** | First number encountered shifts subsequent price estimates 20-50% toward the anchor | Tversky & Kahneman, 1974; Ariely, Loewenstein & Prelec, 2003 | Show "was $X" before discounted price. Show enterprise tier before growth tier. | Anchor must be plausible. Implausible anchors (crossed-out $10,000 on a $47 ebook) trigger suspicion and REDUCE purchase intent (Wegener et al., 2001) |
| **Decoy Effect** | Adding an asymmetrically dominated option shifted preference from 32% to 62% toward the target option | Ariely, 2008 (Predictably Irrational). Original: Huber, Payne & Puck, 1982 | In Good-Better-Best pricing, the "Better" tier is the decoy that makes "Best" look like a deal. The decoy must be clearly inferior to one option but not the other | Effect weakens when consumers are highly knowledgeable about the category (Simonson, 2014). Expert buyers see through decoy pricing |
| **Charm Pricing** | Prices ending in 9 increase sales 8-24% vs round numbers | Anderson & Simester, 2003 (Demand estimation, MIT) | $49 vs $50, $199 vs $200. Works strongest for new/unfamiliar products where price is a key evaluation criterion | Round prices ($100, $200) signal higher quality for luxury/premium. Charm pricing on premium products REDUCES purchase intent (Stiving & Winer, 2007) |
| **Rule of 100** | For items under $100, percentage discounts feel larger. Over $100, absolute discounts feel larger | Gonzalez et al., 2016; widely cited by Jonah Berger | "$29 item: '30% off' beats '$8.70 off'. $500 item: '$150 off' beats '30% off'" | Dual framing (showing both % and $) reduces the effect by introducing comparison |
| **Zero-Price Effect** | Adding a free option increases selection of that option by 4-5x beyond what price reduction alone would predict | Shampanier, Mazar & Ariely, 2007 | Free trials, free tiers, "buy 2 get 1 free". The word "free" triggers a qualitatively different evaluation, not just a lower price | Free creates demand from non-buyers, not just converted demand from price-sensitive buyers. If your goal is qualified leads, free may attract wrong segment |
| **Price-Quality Inference** | 30-40% of consumers use price as a quality signal for experience goods (wine, services, software) | Shiv, Carmon & Ariely, 2005 (wine taste study: $90 wine rated higher than same wine at $5) | Raising prices on premium products can INCREASE perceived quality and willingness to pay. Underpricing signals low quality | Effect disappears for search goods (commodity products with easily comparable specs). Only works for experience/credence goods |

### Social Influence

| Effect | Key Finding | Source | Marketing Application | Boundary Condition |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Social Proof (Descriptive Norms)** | Hotel towel reuse increased 26% with "most guests reuse towels" sign vs standard environmental message | Goldstein, Cialdini & Griskevicius, 2008 | "Join 10,000+ companies" or "Most customers choose the Pro plan" | Social proof BACKFIRES when the group is perceived as dissimilar. "Most people in this building reuse towels" (+33%) outperformed "most hotel guests" (+26%). Irrelevant reference groups reduce trust |
| **Scarcity** | Cookies from a jar of 2 rated significantly more desirable than identical cookies from a jar of 10 | Worchel, Lee & Adewole, 1975. Marketing context: Aggarwal, Jun & Huh, 2011: 32% purchase increase | Limited-time offers, "only X left", cohort-based access. The scarcity must be genuine. | Perceived manipulation cancels the effect entirely (Eisend, 2008). Consumers who detect artificial scarcity rate the product AND the brand lower than no-scarcity control |
| **Authority** | Expert endorsement increases persuasion 14-20% vs non-expert | Hovland & Weiss, 1951; modern replication: Pornpitakpan, 2004 meta-analysis | Expert testimonials, certifications, "recommended by", industry awards | Authority from a mismatched domain has near-zero effect. A doctor endorsing software, a chef endorsing financial products -- the authority must be relevant to the purchase decision |
| **Reciprocity** | Free sample recipients were 42% more likely to purchase vs control | Regan, 1971. Marketing context: numerous field experiments | Free trials, valuable content, free tools, unexpected bonuses post-purchase | Reciprocity decays rapidly with time (within hours, not days). The ask must come close to the gift. Also: the gift must feel voluntary, not transactional ("I gave you this so you'd buy") |
| **Bandwagon Effect** | Knowing a song was popular increased downloads 8x in experimental music market | Salganik, Dodds & Watts, 2006 (Columbia Music Lab) | "Trending", "Most popular", bestseller badges, user count displays | Bandwagon effect is strongest for ambiguous-quality products. For products with clearly verifiable quality, the effect shrinks because individual evaluation overrides social signal |

### Loss & Risk

| Effect | Key Finding | Source | Marketing Application | Boundary Condition |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Loss Aversion** | Losses feel 1.5-2.5x as painful as equivalent gains feel pleasurable | Kahneman & Tversky, 1979 (Prospect Theory). Ratio debated: Gal & Rucker 2018 argue ~1.5x, not 2x | "Don't miss out", "You'll lose access", "Your savings expire". Frame the alternative as a loss, not the product as a gain | Loss aversion DECREASES for very small amounts (<$5) and very large amounts (>$10,000). The 1.5-2.5x multiplier holds most reliably in the $50-$5,000 range for consumer decisions |
| **Endowment Effect** | Owners valued items 2-3x higher than buyers were willing to pay | Kahneman, Knetsch & Thaler, 1990 (coffee mug study) | Free trials create ownership ("your dashboard", "your data"). Trial-to-paid conversion leverages endowment | Endowment effect is weaker for digital goods than physical goods (Atasoy & Morewedge, 2018). Personalizing the trial experience ("your" dashboard, "your" workspace) strengthens it for digital |
| **Status Quo Bias** | Default organ donation (opt-out) achieves 90%+ participation vs 15-20% for opt-in | Johnson & Goldstein, 2003 | Default plan selection, pre-selected options, auto-renewal. The default is the most powerful choice architecture lever | Defaults that feel deceptive (pre-checked add-ons, hidden auto-renewal) trigger backlash AND regulatory action (EU DSA, FTC). The default must serve the user's likely preference |
| **Sunk Cost** | MBA students with a $15 ticket attended a worse play over a $5 ticket play they preferred | Arkes & Blumer, 1985 | Time/effort invested in onboarding reduces churn ("you've already set up 3 integrations"). Progress indicators leverage both sunk cost and goal-gradient | Sunk cost influence is weaker for recurring subscriptions than one-time purchases. Monthly subscribers re-evaluate each billing cycle; the "already invested" argument needs reinforcement |

### Cognitive Processing

| Effect | Key Finding | Source | Marketing Application | Boundary Condition |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Paradox of Choice** | Jam display with 24 options: 3% purchase rate. 6 options: 30% purchase rate. 10x conversion difference | Iyengar & Lepper, 2000 (Columbia jam study) | Limit visible options. Use filters/categories rather than flat lists. Recommend a default | Recent meta-analysis (Scheibehenne et al., 2010) found the effect is inconsistent. It's strongest when: (1) options are similar, (2) preference is unclear, (3) evaluation is difficult. For expert buyers with clear preferences, more options can increase satisfaction |
| **Goal-Gradient Effect** | Loyalty card with 12 stamps (2 pre-filled) completed faster than 10-stamp card, despite same remaining effort | Kivetz, Urminsky & Zheng, 2006 | Pre-fill progress bars, show completed onboarding steps, "You're 60% done". Effort PERCEPTION matters more than actual effort | The acceleration effect (working harder as goal approaches) means the last 20% of a checklist gets completed 2x faster than the first 20%. Front-load easy steps to build momentum |
| **Peak-End Rule** | Experiences are remembered primarily by their peak moment and their ending, not their average | Kahneman et al., 1993 (cold water study) | Customer experience: make the last interaction excellent (post-purchase email, unboxing). Make the peak moment shareable (surprise upgrade, handwritten note) | The peak-end rule means a terrible middle can be forgiven if the peak and end are strong. But a terrible END overshadows a great middle. Cancellation experiences, return processes, and final emails matter disproportionately |
| **Mere Exposure Effect** | Repeated exposure to neutral stimuli increases liking by 10-30% | Zajonc, 1968; advertising meta-analysis: Schmidt & Eisend, 2015 | Retargeting, consistent brand presence across channels, Rule of 7 (7+ exposures before purchase). Familiarity breeds preference | Over-exposure reverses the effect (wear-out). The optimal frequency is 10-20 exposures before diminishing returns. Beyond 20, irritation increases (Schmidt & Eisend, 2015) |
