# Performance Management Framework Reference

## Goal-Setting Frameworks: When Each Fits

| Framework | Best For | Structure | Review Cadence |
|-----------|----------|-----------|----------------|
| OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) | Strategic alignment, cross-functional teams, product/engineering | 1 Objective + 3-5 Key Results per quarter. Objectives are qualitative and ambitious. Key Results are quantitative and measurable. | Quarterly |
| KPIs (Key Performance Indicators) | Operational roles, steady-state functions (support, sales, ops) | 3-5 standing metrics tracked continuously. Targets reset annually. | Monthly tracking, quarterly review |
| MBOs (Management by Objectives) | Executive and senior leadership, complex multi-quarter initiatives | 3-4 negotiated objectives per year, jointly set by manager and employee. Weighted by importance. | Semi-annual or annual |

**Decision rule:** Use OKRs when the work is changing and exploratory. Use KPIs when the work is repetitive and optimizable. Use MBOs when outcomes are complex and require judgment.

## SMART Goals Template

Every goal, regardless of framework, passes the SMART filter:

| Element | Test | Bad Example | Good Example |
|---------|------|-------------|--------------|
| Specific | Can someone else understand exactly what "done" looks like? | "Improve customer satisfaction" | "Increase CSAT score from 72 to 80" |
| Measurable | Is there a number or observable outcome? | "Be more responsive" | "Reduce average ticket response time from 4 hours to 2 hours" |
| Achievable | Is this within the employee's control and capacity? | "Double revenue single-handedly" | "Generate $50K in pipeline from outbound prospecting" |
| Relevant | Does this connect to team or company priorities? | "Learn a new programming language" (if unrelated to role) | "Complete AWS certification to support cloud migration project" |
| Time-bound | Is there a deadline? | "Eventually launch the feature" | "Ship MVP to beta users by March 31" |

## Continuous Feedback Mechanisms

### 1:1 Meeting Structure

**Cadence:** Weekly (for new hires and ICs) or biweekly (for tenured employees and managers). 30 minutes. Never cancel -- reschedule if needed. Canceling 1:1s signals the employee's development is not a priority.

**Agenda ownership:** The employee owns the agenda. The manager listens, coaches, and unblocks. A manager-dominated 1:1 is a status update, not a development conversation.

**Standing questions:**
1. "What's going well this week?"
2. "What's blocking you or frustrating you?"
3. "How can I help?"
4. "Is there anything you need that you're not getting?"

**Manager prep:** Review the employee's recent work, any metrics dashboards, and notes from the last 1:1. Arrive informed. "What have you been working on?" as an opening question signals that the manager has not been paying attention.

### Skip-Level Meetings

**Cadence:** Quarterly. 30 minutes. The manager's manager meets with individual contributors directly.

**Purpose:** Surface issues that do not travel through the management chain. Build trust across organizational levels. Identify systemic problems (workload imbalance, tooling gaps, cultural drift).

**Ground rules:** Skip-levels are not for performance evaluation or gossip about the direct manager. Frame as: "I want to understand how things are going at the team level so I can support [your manager] better."

### 360 Reviews

**Cadence:** Annual. Collect written feedback from 4-6 reviewers: 2 peers, 1-2 cross-functional partners, direct reports (for managers), and the direct manager.

**Critical rule:** Use 360 data for development planning only. Never tie 360 feedback to compensation or promotion decisions. When employees know 360 data affects their pay, feedback becomes political and useless. Separate development conversations from compensation conversations by at least 30 days.

## Formal Review Cycle

### Quarterly Lightweight Review (30 min)

**Template (1 page):**
1. Goal progress: On track / Behind / Ahead -- for each active goal
2. Key accomplishments this quarter
3. Development focus for next quarter
4. Manager feedback: 1 strength to continue, 1 area to develop
5. Employee self-assessment: same format

### Annual Comprehensive Review (60-90 min)

**Template (2-3 pages):**
1. Year-in-review: major accomplishments against annual goals
2. Competency assessment: rate each role competency on 1-4 scale with evidence
3. Development plan: skills to build, experiences to seek, support needed
4. Career conversation: where does the employee want to be in 1-2 years?
5. Overall performance rating (see rating scale below)
6. Compensation discussion (separate meeting, 2+ weeks later)

## Rating Scale: Why 4-Point Outperforms 5-Point

| Rating | Label | Definition |
|--------|-------|-----------|
| 1 | Does Not Meet Expectations | Significant gaps in core responsibilities. PIP territory. |
| 2 | Partially Meets Expectations | Meets some requirements but has clear areas needing improvement. Development plan required. |
| 3 | Meets Expectations | Delivers on all core responsibilities at the expected level. This is the target for most employees. |
| 4 | Exceeds Expectations | Consistently delivers above role requirements. Demonstrates next-level capabilities. |

A 5-point scale produces a "3 = meets expectations" middle where 70%+ of employees cluster, making the scale useless for differentiation. A 4-point scale forces a meaningful distinction: "partially meets" (improvement needed) vs "meets" (solid performance). There is no comfortable middle.

**Distribution guidance (not forced ranking):** Expect approximately 10% at 1-2, 65% at 3, 25% at 4. If more than 30% of a team is rated 4, the manager's bar is too low. If more than 20% is rated 1-2, there is a systemic team problem (hiring, management, or role design).

## Calibration Sessions

**Purpose:** Ensure consistent standards across teams and managers. A "3" on Team A should mean the same as a "3" on Team B.

**Process:**
1. Each manager presents their ratings with evidence for each employee
2. Cross-team managers challenge ratings: "What evidence supports a 4 here?"
3. HR facilitates and tracks distribution patterns
4. Adjust ratings where evidence does not support the score
5. Document adjustments and reasoning

**Frequency:** Annual, after review completion but before ratings are communicated to employees. Never change a rating without informing the employee and providing the rationale.

## Compensation Linkage

| Rating | Merit Increase Range | Promotion Eligibility | Equity Refresh |
|--------|---------------------|----------------------|----------------|
| 1 | 0% (PIP initiated) | No | No |
| 2 | 0-2% | No | No |
| 3 | 3-5% | Yes (if meets promotion criteria) | Standard grant |
| 4 | 5-8% | Priority consideration | Accelerated grant |

**Promotion criteria (separate from performance rating):** The employee consistently operates at the next level for 2+ quarters. A high rating at the current level does not automatically mean readiness for promotion. Promotion requires demonstrated scope expansion, not just excellence in current scope.

## PIP Structure

### When to Use a PIP

Use a PIP when: specific, documented performance deficiencies exist AND the employee has received prior feedback AND you believe improvement is genuinely achievable.

Do NOT use a PIP when: the decision to terminate is already made (that is **The Perpetual PIP**), the issue is role fit rather than performance, or the problem is behavioral/ethical rather than skill-based.

### PIP Template

1. **Specific deficiencies:** List 2-4 concrete, observable performance gaps with dates and examples. "Your close rate was 12% in Q1 against a 25% target" -- not "You need to sell more."
2. **Expected standards:** Define what success looks like with measurable targets. "Achieve a 20% close rate for each of the next 3 months."
3. **Support plan:** What the company will provide: additional training, mentoring, reduced quota, tooling. A PIP without support is a setup for failure.
4. **Timeline:** 30 days for tactical roles, 60 days for complex roles, 90 days for leadership roles. Weekly check-ins throughout.
5. **Consequences:** "If these targets are not met by [date], we will transition you out of this role." Direct language. No ambiguity.

### PIP Success Benchmarks

Industry average PIP success rate: 20-30%. If your success rate is below 10%, your PIPs are likely pretextual. If above 50%, your bar for initiating PIPs may be too low (the issues might be better addressed through coaching alone).

## Manager Training: The SBI Feedback Model

**S (Situation):** Describe the specific context. "In yesterday's client presentation..."
**B (Behavior):** Describe the observable behavior. "...you interrupted the client three times during their questions..."
**I (Impact):** Describe the effect. "...which caused them to stop asking questions, and we missed signals about their real concerns."

SBI separates observation from judgment. "You were rude in the meeting" is judgment. SBI is evidence. Managers must practice SBI until it becomes reflexive -- most feedback failures stem from leading with judgment instead of observation.

## Documentation Requirements

| Stage | What to Document | Where to Store |
|-------|-----------------|----------------|
| Goal setting | Written goals with SMART criteria and manager sign-off | HRIS or shared doc, accessible to employee and manager |
| 1:1 meetings | Brief notes on key discussion points and action items | Manager's private notes (share action items with employee) |
| Quarterly reviews | Completed 1-page review template | HRIS or HR-managed folder |
| Annual reviews | Completed comprehensive review, signed by both parties | HRIS with permanent retention |
| PIPs | Full PIP document, weekly check-in notes, outcome record | HR-managed folder with restricted access |
| Terminations | Complete documentation trail: warnings, PIP, final decision memo | HR-managed folder, retained per legal requirements (typically 7 years) |
