# Headline Formulas, CTA Templates, and Copy Patterns

## Headline Formulas

Each formula includes WHEN it works and WHEN it fails. Pick based on awareness stage and traffic source.

### Problem-Aware Headlines (Visitor knows the pain, not your solution)

**{Achieve desirable outcome} without {pain point}**
- Works: When the audience has a specific, named frustration
- Fails: When the pain point is vague or the audience doesn't feel urgency
- Example: "Understand how users experience your site without drowning in numbers"

**Never {unpleasant event} again**
- Works: High-frequency annoyances where the audience is fed up
- Fails: Low-stakes problems or B2B enterprise where "never again" sounds hyperbolic
- Example: "Never miss a sales opportunity again"

**{Question highlighting the main pain point}**
- Works: When the pain is visceral and widely shared -- hooks via recognition
- Fails: When the question has an easy "no" answer or sounds rhetorical
- Example: "Hate returning stuff to Amazon?"

**Stop {pain}. Start {pleasure}.**
- Works: Clear before/after contrast where both states are concrete
- Fails: When either side is abstract ("Stop struggling. Start succeeding.")
- Example: "Stop chasing invoices. Start getting paid on time."

### Solution-Aware Headlines (Visitor knows solutions exist, evaluating options)

**The {opposite of usual process} way to {achieve desirable outcome}**
- Works: Crowded categories where differentiation matters most
- Fails: New categories where there is no "usual process" to contrast
- Example: "The easiest way to turn your passion into income"

**{Key feature/product type} for {target audience}**
- Works: When the audience self-identifies strongly (by role, industry, company size)
- Fails: When the audience is broad or the feature is generic
- Example: "Advanced analytics for Shopify e-commerce"

**{Key feature/product type} for {target audience} to {what it's used for}**
- Works: Complex products where use-case clarity drives conversion
- Fails: Simple products where the extra clause adds unnecessary length
- Example: "An online whiteboard for teams to ideate and brainstorm together"

**The {category} that {key differentiator}**
- Works: When you have ONE clear, provable differentiator
- Fails: When the differentiator is subjective ("the CRM that cares")
- Example: "The project tracker that updates itself"

### Most-Aware Headlines (Visitor knows your product, needs a push)

**{Achieve desirable outcome} by {how product makes it possible}**
- Works: When the mechanism is unique and interesting
- Fails: When the mechanism is boring or expected
- Example: "Generate more leads by seeing which companies visit your site"

**{Number} {people} use {product} to {outcome}**
- Works: When the number is impressive and the social proof is genuine
- Fails: When the number is small or the outcome is vague
- Example: "35,000 marketers use Sparktoro to find their audience"

**Turn {input} into {outcome}**
- Works: Transformation products where input/output are concrete
- Fails: When the transformation is incremental rather than dramatic
- Example: "Turn your hard-earned sales into repeat customers"

### Unaware Headlines (Cold traffic, no context)

**You don't have to {skills or resources} to {achieve desirable outcome}**
- Works: When the barrier to entry is widely assumed
- Fails: When the audience already has the skills -- it feels condescending
- Example: "You don't have to be an SEO pro to rank higher and get more traffic"

**{Key benefit of your product}**
- Works: When the benefit is surprising or counterintuitive on its own
- Fails: When the benefit is expected -- just becomes wallpaper
- Example: "Sound clear in online meetings"

**[Achieve outcome] in [timeframe]**
- Works: When the timeframe is genuinely surprising and provable
- Fails: When it sounds like a get-rich-quick claim without proof
- Example: "Launch your online store in 30 minutes"

## CTA Templates

### Strong CTA Patterns

**[Action Verb] + [What They Get] + [Qualifier]**
- "Start My Free Trial"
- "Get the Complete Checklist"
- "See Pricing for My Team"

**[First-Person Possessive] CTAs (higher conversion in tests)**
- "Start My Free Trial" > "Start Your Free Trial"
- "Book My Demo" > "Book a Demo"
- "Get My Report" > "Get the Report"

### CTA Selection by Funnel Stage

| Awareness Stage | CTA Style | Examples |
|----------------|-----------|----------|
| Unaware | Low-commitment, curiosity | "See How It Works", "Watch the Demo" |
| Problem-aware | Educational | "Get the Free Guide", "Take the Assessment" |
| Solution-aware | Evaluative | "Compare Plans", "See It in Action" |
| Product-aware | Trial/proof | "Start Free Trial", "Try It Free" |
| Most-aware | Purchase/commit | "Get Started Now", "Upgrade My Plan" |

### CTAs to NEVER Use (and Why)

| Weak CTA | Why It Fails | Better Alternative |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Submit | Zero value communicated | "Get My Results" |
| Sign Up | Focuses on what they DO, not GET | "Create My Free Account" |
| Learn More | Vague, no commitment signal | "See How [Product] Works" |
| Click Here | Describes the mechanics, not the value | "Download the Guide" |
| Get Started | Generic, no specificity | "Start My Free Trial" |
| Contact Us | Puts effort on the visitor | "Book a 15-Min Call" |

## Subheadline Patterns

The subheadline has ONE job: fill the gap the headline left.

| If Headline Is... | Subheadline Should... | Example |
|-------------------|----------------------|---------|
| Benefit-focused | Add specificity/mechanism | H: "Close deals faster" / Sub: "AI analyzes your pipeline and surfaces the 3 deals most likely to close this week" |
| Curiosity-driven | Deliver the answer | H: "What if your CRM updated itself?" / Sub: "Dropcontact enriches every record automatically so your team sells, not types" |
| Audience-targeted | Add the benefit | H: "Built for Shopify stores doing $1M+" / Sub: "Advanced analytics that show you exactly where revenue leaks" |
| Short/punchy | Expand with detail | H: "Sound clear." / Sub: "Krisp removes background noise from your calls so you never ask 'can you repeat that?' again" |

## Copy Micro-Patterns

### The "So What" Test
After every claim, ask "so what?" The answer is your actual copy.
- Claim: "AI-powered analytics" -- So what?
- Better: "See which pages lose customers before they buy"

### The Proof Sandwich
Claim -> Proof -> Implication
- "Teams save 4 hours per week (based on 2,000 customer survey) -- that's a full month back every year."

### The Objection Preempt
State the objection before the reader thinks it, then resolve it.
- "You might think another tool means another learning curve. Most teams are up and running in under 10 minutes."

### Voice-of-Customer Injection
Lift exact phrases from reviews, support tickets, and interviews. Put them in headlines and body copy. Readers recognize their own language and feel understood.
- Review says: "I was drowning in spreadsheets"
- Headline: "Stop drowning in spreadsheets"
