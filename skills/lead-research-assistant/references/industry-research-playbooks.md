# Industry-Specific Research Playbooks

## SaaS B2B (< $20K ACV)

### Research Priority
Speed over depth. The goal is to qualify or disqualify fast and get to outreach within the same session. These deals close in 1-4 weeks if the fit is right.

### Key Research Steps
1. **Tech stack check** (BuiltWith + job postings) -- do they use tools adjacent to yours? If selling a CI/CD tool, are they already using GitHub Actions, Jenkins, etc.?
2. **Competitor check** -- are they using a direct competitor? If yes, look for dissatisfaction signals (G2 reviews, community complaints). If no competitor, assess whether they've identified the need at all.
3. **Team size in the relevant department** -- LinkedIn. For SMB SaaS, the department size predicts deal size and urgency.
4. **Recent changes** -- new hiring manager, new VP, funding in last 12 months. Any change = evaluation window.

### Decision Maker Identification
- 1-50 employees: Founder/CEO decides everything. Target them directly.
- 51-200 employees: Department head (VP/Director level) decides tools. CEO approves budget for anything > $10K.
- 201-500 employees: Department head decides and usually has budget authority. Procurement may gate but doesn't decide.

### Scoring Emphasis
Weight tech stack fit (base score) and active evaluation signals (intent) highest. Budget is rarely a blocker at this deal size -- if they want it, they'll find $200-1,500/month.

### Outreach Angle
Lead with the specific tech integration or workflow improvement. "I noticed you're using [X tool] -- [your product] plugs directly into that workflow and [specific benefit]." Generic outreach fails at this level because buyers are sophisticated and see through it.

## Enterprise B2B (> $50K ACV)

### Research Priority
Depth over speed. One well-researched enterprise lead is worth 50 poorly researched ones. These deals take 3-12 months and require multi-stakeholder buy-in.

### Key Research Steps
1. **Strategic priorities** -- annual report, earnings calls, CEO interviews, press releases. What does the company SAY it's focused on this year?
2. **Org mapping** -- identify 3-5 stakeholders minimum: economic buyer (budget), technical evaluator, end user, champion, blocker. LinkedIn is primary source.
3. **Decision process** -- does this company have a procurement department? (Check LinkedIn for "procurement" or "vendor management" roles.) If yes, add 30-60 days to any timeline estimate.
4. **Budget cycle timing** -- most enterprises plan budgets Q4 for next fiscal year. If you're pitching in Q3, you're likely too late for this year's budget. Research their fiscal year end date.
5. **Incumbent analysis** -- what do they currently use? How deeply integrated is it? What's the switching cost? If switching cost > 2x your annual contract value, the deal economics don't work unless there's strong pain.
6. **Champion identification** -- who inside the org has spoken publicly about the problem you solve? Conference talks, blog posts, LinkedIn articles, podcast appearances.

### Decision Maker Identification
- Enterprise buying is NEVER one person. Map the buying committee:
  - **Economic Buyer:** VP/SVP/C-level who owns the budget line item. Usually 2 levels above the end user.
  - **Technical Evaluator:** The person who will assess whether your product actually works. Usually a senior IC or team lead.
  - **End User:** The person who will use the product daily. Their opinion matters but they rarely have budget authority.
  - **Champion:** Someone inside who actively wants your solution and will sell internally on your behalf.
  - **Blocker:** IT security, procurement, legal. Know who they are so you can prepare for their objections.

### Scoring Emphasis
Weight champion existence and decision process clarity highest. An enterprise lead without a plausible champion path is a 5 at best, regardless of fit. Add +2 for identified champion, -2 for unknown decision process.

### Outreach Angle
Lead with strategic alignment to their stated priorities. Reference specific public statements from leadership. "In your Q3 earnings call, [CEO name] mentioned [priority]. We've helped [similar company] achieve [specific metric] in that area." Enterprise buyers respond to demonstrated knowledge of their business, not product features.

## Professional Services (Consulting, Agency, Legal, Accounting)

### Research Priority
Relationship proximity and trigger events. Professional services firms buy from people they know or people who come recommended. Cold outreach has the lowest conversion rate of any segment.

### Key Research Steps
1. **Trigger event scan** -- new office opening, new practice area launch, key hire, major client win (or loss), regulatory change affecting their practice. These create buying moments.
2. **Relationship mapping** -- do you share any connections? Alumni networks, industry associations, conference attendance. One warm introduction = 10x cold outreach response rate.
3. **Service delivery model** -- are they project-based or retainer-based? Project-based firms buy tools that accelerate delivery. Retainer firms buy tools that improve client reporting/communication.
4. **Client concentration** -- if they're heavily dependent on one industry, your product needs to be relevant to that industry's needs, not just the firm's internal operations.

### Decision Maker Identification
- Small firms (< 50): Managing Partner decides. Period.
- Mid-size (50-500): Practice leaders decide for their practice. Managing Partner approves anything firm-wide.
- Large (500+): Functions like enterprise. Procurement exists. Budget approval is committee-driven.

### Scoring Emphasis
Weight trigger events and relationship proximity highest. A professional services firm with perfect ICP match but no trigger event and no relationship path is a 3 -- the deal will take 12+ months of relationship building.

### Outreach Angle
Lead with the trigger event and a mutual connection if possible. "I noticed you recently launched your [new practice area]. [Mutual connection] mentioned you might be looking at [category]. We've worked with [similar firm] on exactly this." If no mutual connection, reference a specific trigger event and offer insight, not a pitch.

## SMB / Local Business

### Research Priority
Owner accessibility and growth signals. SMB buying decisions happen fast (1-7 days) because one person decides. The challenge is finding SMBs that are growing (and therefore investing) vs. stable (and therefore not buying).

### Key Research Steps
1. **Online presence quality** -- a well-maintained website with recent blog posts suggests a business that invests in growth. A neglected site suggests a lifestyle business unlikely to buy tools.
2. **Google reviews trajectory** -- increasing reviews = growing business. Complaints about "slow service" or "hard to reach" = operational pain your product might solve.
3. **Social media activity** -- active social media (especially paid ads visible via Facebook Ad Library) = marketing investment = growth orientation.
4. **Location count / expansion** -- multiple locations or recent expansion = operational complexity that creates tool needs.
5. **Owner background** -- LinkedIn. A tech-savvy owner adopts tools faster. A traditional owner needs more education and proof.

### Decision Maker Identification
- The owner. Always. Sometimes a "Director of Operations" or "Office Manager" for operational tools, but the owner approves the spend.
- If you can't identify the owner by name, the lead is under-researched.

### Scoring Emphasis
Weight growth signals and owner accessibility highest. A growing SMB with an accessible, tech-savvy owner who has an active pain point is a 9-10. A stable SMB with a hard-to-reach owner is a 2 regardless of "fit."

### Outreach Angle
Lead with local relevance and specific observation. "I saw your [specific detail -- new location, recent hire, Google review response]. We help [type of business] in [location] with [specific outcome]." SMB owners respond to specificity and local knowledge. Generic outreach gets deleted.

## Cross-Context Rules

These apply regardless of which playbook you're using:

1. **Timing beats fit.** A mediocre-fit lead with a trigger event happening NOW outscores a perfect-fit lead with no urgency. Weight timing signals accordingly.

2. **One good angle beats five generic ones.** Find ONE specific, defensible reason this lead should talk to you. Put it first in any outreach recommendation.

3. **Negative signals are more reliable than positive signals.** A company actively laying off the department you sell to is definitely a bad lead. A company hiring in that department is probably a good lead but could be replacing, not expanding. Trust disqualifying signals more than qualifying ones.

4. **Research has diminishing returns.** The first 10 minutes of research on a lead captures 80% of the actionable intelligence. The next 30 minutes captures 15%. The next 60 minutes captures 5%. Know when to stop and move to the next lead.
