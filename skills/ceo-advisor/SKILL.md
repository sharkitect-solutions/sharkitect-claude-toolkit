# CEO Advisor

## Rationalization Table -- Common Mistakes When Advising CEOs

| What the CEO says | What they actually need | Why the obvious answer is wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "Help me write a strategic plan" | A decision about what to STOP doing | Most CEOs have too many initiatives, not too few. Strategy is saying no. |
| "I need to hire a VP of Sales" | Diagnosis of why current sales are broken | A VP hire into a broken system produces an expensive firing in 9 months. |
| "Should I raise a Series A?" | Clarity on whether the business model works | Raising before product-market fit buys runway but delays the reckoning. |
| "My board is difficult" | A pre-wiring and communication discipline | Boards become "difficult" when they learn bad news in the meeting instead of before it. |
| "We need to move faster" | Fewer priorities, not more people | Adding headcount to a confused organization makes it slower, not faster. |
| "Help me prepare for the board meeting" | Identification of the 1-2 decisions the board should actually make | Board packages that inform without requesting decisions waste everyone's time. |
| "I need a crisis plan" | Immediate triage -- is this actually a crisis? | 80% of "crises" are fire drills. Real crises have existential consequences within 90 days. |
| "We need to restructure" | Diagnosis of whether the problem is structure or people | Reorgs that move boxes without changing who is in the boxes change nothing. |

## NEVER List -- CEO Decision Anti-Patterns

1. **Never surprise your board.** If a board member learns material bad news during the meeting, you have already failed. Pre-wire every significant issue with individual calls 1-2 weeks before. Why: surprised boards become hostile boards.
2. **Never raise capital when you desperately need it.** Raise when you have 12+ months of runway and strong metrics. Desperation fundraising produces terrible terms and signals weakness. Why: VCs smell desperation and price accordingly.
3. **Never hire senior leaders to "figure it out."** If you cannot articulate the specific problem the hire solves and the measurable outcome you expect in 6 months, you are not ready to hire. Why: expensive hires without clear mandates become expensive political actors.
4. **Never keep a mis-hire past 90 days hoping they will improve.** The data is clear: executives who are not performing at 90 days almost never become strong performers. Every month you wait costs the team more than it costs you. Why: the team knows before you do, and your credibility erodes daily.
5. **Never confuse motion with progress.** More meetings, more hires, more initiatives, more decks -- none of these are results. If you cannot point to a customer outcome or financial metric that moved, the activity was waste. Why: CEO activity is contagious. If you are busy with low-value work, your whole organization mirrors it.
6. **Never make irreversible decisions under emotional pressure.** Firing someone in anger, accepting an acquisition offer the same day, shutting down a product line after one bad quarter. Sleep on any decision that cannot be undone. Why: the CEO's emotional state propagates through the entire company within hours.
7. **Never hide bad news from investors hoping to fix it first.** The problem always gets worse before you disclose, and then you have two problems: the original issue plus broken trust. Why: investors tolerate bad news. They do not tolerate surprises.
8. **Never let a single customer represent more than 25% of revenue without a deliberate concentration strategy.** Customer concentration risk is the most common cause of startup collapse that nobody talks about until it happens. Why: when that customer churns or renegotiates, you face an existential crisis with no time to react.
9. **Never skip the reference check on senior hires.** Not the references the candidate provides -- the back-channel ones. Call 5 people who worked with them who are NOT on their list. Why: provided references are marketing. Back-channel references are diligence.
10. **Never scale the team before you have a repeatable process.** Hiring people to execute a process that does not exist yet produces chaos, not scale. Why: each new hire in a process vacuum invents their own process, creating organizational entropy.

## CEO Problem Decision Tree

When a CEO brings a problem, classify it FIRST. The category determines the approach.

```
What kind of CEO problem is this?
|
+-- STRATEGY (rare, high-stakes, irreversible)
|   Examples: pivot vs persevere, enter new market, M&A, major partnership
|   Approach: Force a decision deadline. Gather data for 1-2 weeks max.
|   Trap: Analysis paralysis. Strategy decisions degrade with delay.
|   Key question: "What would have to be true for this to work?"
|
+-- PEOPLE (emotional, time-sensitive, high organizational impact)
|   Examples: fire a leader, resolve co-founder conflict, key person leaving
|   Approach: Separate the emotional from the operational. Decide the "what"
|   before the "how." Act within days, not weeks.
|   Trap: Avoiding the conversation. Every day of delay costs team trust.
|   Key question: "If I inherited this team today, would I hire this person?"
|
+-- CAPITAL (irreversible, numbers-driven, long-tail consequences)
|   Examples: fundraise, major investment, pricing change, cost restructuring
|   Approach: Model 3 scenarios (base, upside, downside). Decide based on
|   the downside you can survive, not the upside you hope for.
|   Trap: Optimism bias. CEOs systematically overestimate revenue timelines.
|   Key question: "Can we survive the downside scenario for 18 months?"
|
+-- CRISIS (urgent, existential risk, public-facing)
|   Examples: revenue cliff, PR incident, key customer loss, legal threat
|   Approach: See Crisis Taxonomy below. Classify severity first.
|   Trap: Treating every urgent thing as a crisis. Most are fire drills.
|   Key question: "Does this threaten company survival within 90 days?"
```

## Scaling Breakpoints -- What Changes at Each Stage

Company dynamics change fundamentally at specific headcount thresholds. Each transition requires a different CEO, or the same CEO behaving like a different person.

**1 to 10 people (Founder-Doer)**
- CEO does IC work daily. Writing code, closing deals, handling support.
- No management layer. Direct communication with everyone.
- Hire generalists who can wear 5 hats.
- Danger sign: hiring specialists before you have product-market fit.

**10 to 30 people (Founder-Manager)**
- CEO must STOP doing IC work. This is the hardest transition.
- First management layer appears. 2-3 leads reporting to CEO.
- Process needs to exist for the first time: how we hire, how we ship, how we decide.
- Danger sign: CEO still reviewing every PR, approving every expense, attending every standup.
- What breaks: verbal agreements, tribal knowledge, "everyone knows everything" culture.

**30 to 75 people (CEO-Operator)**
- CEO cannot know what everyone is working on. Accept this.
- Need dedicated HR, finance, and ops functions (even if one person each).
- Written communication becomes mandatory. If it is not documented, it does not exist.
- First real org chart. Reporting lines matter now.
- Danger sign: CEO still making every hiring decision, still in every customer call.
- What breaks: culture transmission (new people do not absorb values by osmosis at this size).

**75 to 150 people (CEO-Executive)**
- CEO cannot know everyone's name. This is normal and necessary.
- Need VP-level leaders who can run functions independently.
- Board governance becomes real, not ceremonial.
- Strategic planning must be formalized. Quarterly cycles, annual plans.
- Danger sign: CEO micromanaging VPs, attending team standups, making decisions VPs should make.
- What breaks: speed. Decisions slow down unless you deliberately push authority down.

**150 to 500 people (CEO-Institution-Builder)**
- CEO runs a political organization whether they want to or not.
- Information is filtered before it reaches you. Build skip-level channels.
- Culture requires active maintenance: rituals, stories, reinforcement.
- Need COO or strong operational #2.
- Danger sign: CEO surprised by problems that "everyone knew about."
- What breaks: alignment. Sub-teams develop their own goals that diverge from company goals.

## Board Meeting Anti-Patterns

**The Data Dump (50 slides, no narrative)**
- Symptom: Board members glazing over by slide 15.
- Fix: 5-page narrative memo. 3 decisions to make. Appendix for data.
- Why it happens: CEO equates thoroughness with credibility.
- Why it fails: boards are not here to be informed. They are here to govern.

**The "Everything Is Fine" (hiding problems)**
- Symptom: Board hears only good news. Bad news surfaces from investors or back-channels.
- Fix: Lead with the worst problem. Then present your plan to address it.
- Why it happens: CEO fears losing board confidence.
- Why it fails: boards lose MORE confidence when they discover hidden problems.

**The Surprise Ask (requesting something without pre-wiring)**
- Symptom: CEO asks for a major decision (new funding round, acquisition, pivot) during the meeting.
- Fix: Call each board member individually 2 weeks before. Float the idea. Get reactions.
- Why it happens: CEO wants efficiency -- one meeting, one decision.
- Why it fails: board members need processing time. Surprised boards say no by default.

**The No-Decision Meeting (presenting info without requesting a vote)**
- Symptom: Meeting ends with "any questions?" instead of "I need a vote on X."
- Fix: Every board meeting should have 1-3 explicit decision items on the agenda.
- Why it happens: CEO is not sure what they need from the board.
- Why it fails: boards without decisions to make become disengaged or start micromanaging.

## Fundraising Decision Tree

```
Should you raise external capital?
|
+-- Are unit economics proven (LTV > 3x CAC)?
|   +-- NO --> Do not raise. Fix unit economics first. Raising capital to fund
|   |          negative unit economics buys time but deepens the hole.
|   +-- YES --> Continue.
|
+-- Is your market winner-take-all or winner-take-most?
|   +-- NO --> Consider bootstrapping. If the market supports multiple winners,
|   |          you may not need venture speed. Profitable growth > funded growth.
|   +-- YES --> Raising makes sense. Speed matters. Continue.
|
+-- Do you have 12+ months of runway right now?
|   +-- NO --> Start raising IMMEDIATELY. You are behind.
|   |          Accept: terms will be worse because you have no leverage.
|   +-- YES --> Good position. You have leverage. Be selective.
|
+-- Strategic money or financial money?
    +-- STRATEGIC (corporate VC, industry player):
    |   Good when: you need distribution, partnerships, or domain expertise.
    |   Bad when: the strategic investor is also a potential acquirer or competitor.
    |   Watch for: right of first refusal, information rights that leak to competitors.
    +-- FINANCIAL (traditional VC, growth equity):
        Good when: you need pure capital and operational advice.
        Bad when: the investor has a conflicting portfolio company.
        Watch for: aggressive liquidation preferences, board control provisions.
```

## Crisis Taxonomy

Not all crises are equal. Classify before you act.

**Revenue Crisis (runway under 6 months)**
- Action: CUT, do not optimize. Reduce burn by 30-40% within 2 weeks.
- Common mistake: cutting 10% and hoping revenue recovers. If revenue were going to recover, you would not be in crisis.
- CEO time allocation: 80% on revenue, 20% on everything else.
- Timeline: you have 2 weeks to act. After that, you are planning from a weaker position.

**People Crisis (key person departing, co-founder conflict, culture collapse)**
- Action: CONTAIN first, then fix. Stop the information cascade before it triggers more departures.
- Common mistake: making a public announcement before you have a plan.
- CEO time allocation: clear your calendar for 48 hours. This is now your only job.
- Timeline: contain within 48 hours. Fix over 2-4 weeks.

**Market Crisis (major competitor move, platform risk, regulatory threat)**
- Action: DECIDE within 48 hours, not 48 days. Markets do not wait for your analysis.
- Common mistake: commissioning a study. By the time the study is done, the market has moved.
- CEO time allocation: 2-day war room with your top 3-4 people. Decide and announce.
- Timeline: decision within 48 hours. Execution plan within 1 week.

**Reputation Crisis (PR incident, data breach, public failure)**
- Action: RESPOND in hours, not days. Silence is interpreted as guilt.
- Common mistake: lawyering the response into meaninglessness. Humans detect corporate speak.
- CEO time allocation: you are the spokesperson. Do not delegate this.
- Timeline: initial response within 4 hours. Detailed response within 24 hours.

## The "Founder Mode" Trap

When hands-on is genius:
- Product vision decisions (what to build, what to kill)
- First 3 enterprise sales (nobody sells your product like you)
- Key executive hires (the CEO should close the final candidate)
- Company narrative (fundraising pitch, board narrative, culture story)
- Customer escalations that reveal systemic product problems

When hands-on is pathological:
- Reviewing all pull requests at 50+ engineers
- Approving every expense regardless of amount
- Attending every team standup
- Editing every piece of marketing copy
- Personally handling customer support tickets
- Insisting on being in every interview loop

The test: "Am I doing this because only I can, or because I do not trust my team?"
If it is trust, the problem is your hiring, not your involvement.

## Before/After: CEO Communication Mistake

**BEFORE (bad CEO communication in an all-hands during layoffs):**

"As you know, the macroeconomic environment has presented challenges across the
industry. After careful consideration and strategic review, we have made the
difficult decision to right-size our organization to better position ourselves
for sustainable long-term growth. This restructuring will affect approximately
15% of our workforce. We remain committed to our mission and confident in our
future. HR will be reaching out to affected individuals with details about
severance packages and transition support."

Problems: corporate speak hides responsibility, passive voice removes the CEO
from the decision, "right-size" is a euphemism that insults everyone's
intelligence, no acknowledgment of what the CEO did wrong that led here.

**AFTER (honest CEO communication):**

"I am letting 45 people go this week. I owe you an honest explanation of why.

We grew too fast in Q2 and Q3 based on revenue projections that did not
materialize. That forecasting failure is mine -- I approved the hiring plan.

Here is what happens next. Every affected person gets 12 weeks of severance,
3 months of health insurance, and job placement support starting today. Their
managers are having individual conversations right now.

For everyone staying: our runway is now 18 months. We are not doing another
round of cuts. I will share our revised financial plan at Thursday's team
meeting so you can see the numbers yourselves.

I know trust is earned, not declared. I will answer every question you have."

Why this works: CEO owns the mistake, uses specific numbers, explains what
happens next, and does not hide behind euphemisms or committees.
