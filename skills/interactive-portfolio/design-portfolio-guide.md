# Design Portfolio Guide

## Case Study Depth Calibration

Different reviewers need different depth levels. Design your case studies with layered depth -- fast scan for recruiters, deep dive for design leads.

| Reviewer | Time Spent | What They Check | Depth Needed | Structure |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Recruiter (initial screen) | 30-60 seconds per portfolio | Does candidate match job requirements? Visual quality check. Do projects align with role? | Surface only: thumbnails, titles, one-line summaries, tool/skill tags | Project cards with strong thumbnails and clear metadata. Recruiter should never need to click into a case study |
| Hiring manager (shortlist) | 2-5 minutes per portfolio | Can this person do the work? Evidence of impact? Communication ability? | Medium: skim case studies. Read problem statement and results. Check 2-3 process images | Case study with scannable headers, bold key takeaways, and metrics in highlighted callouts |
| Design lead (portfolio review) | 10-20 minutes for top candidates | HOW does this person think? What's their design process? How do they handle constraints? How do they collaborate? | Full depth: read entire case study. Study process artifacts. Evaluate decision-making rationale | Complete case study with research, ideation, iteration, testing, and reflection sections |
| Design team (culture fit) | 5-10 minutes, focused on 1-2 projects | Would this person elevate our team? Do they bring complementary skills? How do they handle feedback? | Selective depth: skip to the sections that reveal working style | Sections on collaboration, feedback incorporation, and lessons learned |

**Layered structure**: Build case studies with progressive disclosure. Put the hook (problem + result) in the first 200 words. Process details in expandable sections or below the fold. This serves all reviewer types without forcing anyone to scroll past content they don't care about.

## Process Documentation Strategy

Showing process is what separates junior portfolios from senior ones. But showing ALL process is a mistake -- curate the moments that reveal thinking.

| Process Artifact | When to Show | When to Skip | Presentation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Research findings | When research changed your direction. "User interviews revealed X, which made us pivot from Y to Z" | When research confirmed what you already assumed. "We did 5 interviews and they confirmed our hypothesis" adds no value | 3-5 key insights as bullet points or an insight synthesis board. NOT a 20-slide research deck |
| Sketches/wireframes | When they show exploration breadth. "We explored 4 directions before converging on the final approach" | When they're just rough versions of the final design. One wireframe that looks like a less polished final = no insight | Side-by-side comparison of 3-4 explored directions. Annotate why you eliminated alternatives |
| User flows | When the flow itself was the design challenge. Complex multi-step processes, edge cases, error states | For simple, standard flows (login, signup, settings). Everyone knows what a signup flow looks like | Annotated flow with decision points highlighted. Show where you simplified compared to competitors |
| Design system components | When you built or significantly extended a design system. Shows systematic thinking | When you used an existing design system without modification. Using Material Design isn't portfolio-worthy | Component library screenshot with naming convention. Show the system's logic, not every button variant |
| Usability test results | When testing revealed non-obvious problems. "4 of 5 users couldn't find the export button" | When testing confirmed everything worked. "Users found the flow intuitive" is not a finding | Before/after comparison. Show the specific problem test revealed and your design solution |
| Iteration evolution | When significant iteration happened. Show version 1, key feedback, version 2, key feedback, final | When changes were minor. Showing 10 nearly-identical iterations suggests indecision, not rigor | 3 versions max: early exploration, pivotal iteration, final design. Annotate what changed and why |

## Design System Showcase

If you've built or contributed to a design system, it's one of the most valuable portfolio pieces for mid-senior roles.

| Element to Showcase | Why It Matters | How to Present |
|---|---|---|
| Token architecture | Shows you think systematically about scale. Colors, spacing, typography as a system, not one-off decisions | Visual hierarchy: show the token scale and how tokens compose into components. "8px base unit -> 4/8/12/16/24/32/48 spacing scale" |
| Component hierarchy | Atoms -> molecules -> organisms thinking. Shows you understand composition and reusability | Pick 1 complex component (e.g., data table) and decompose it: base cell -> row -> header -> table -> filterable table |
| Documentation approach | Design systems fail without documentation. Showing you documented yours signals maturity | Screenshot of component documentation. Show usage guidelines, do/don't examples, and accessibility notes |
| Governance model | Who can change what? How are contributions reviewed? | Describe the process, not just the output. "I established a weekly design system review meeting and contribution guidelines that reduced inconsistency by 40%" |

## Multi-Platform Work Display

Showing work across platforms (web, mobile, tablet, wearable) requires careful presentation to avoid visual clutter.

| Approach | Best For | Implementation |
|---|---|---|
| Device mockup grid | Responsive web design showing how the same design adapts across breakpoints | 3 devices (mobile, tablet, desktop) in a single horizontal image. Use consistent mockup frames. Figma plugins: Mockup or Device Frames |
| Platform comparison | Native iOS vs Android design for the same feature | Side-by-side showing platform-specific patterns (iOS navigation bar vs Android top app bar, iOS bottom tabs vs Android navigation drawer). Annotate WHY they differ |
| Flow across devices | Cross-device user journey (start on mobile, continue on desktop) | Storyboard-style: numbered steps showing the handoff between devices. Highlight continuity in the experience |
| Single hero device | When one platform was the primary design focus | Show the primary platform at full size. Other platforms as smaller supporting images below or in a "Also designed for..." section |

## Presenting UX Research in Portfolio

Research skills are increasingly valuable but poorly presented in most portfolios.

| Research Method | How to Present | What It Proves About You |
|---|---|---|
| User interviews | Key quotes (2-3 most impactful) -> Insight synthesis -> Design implication. NOT a transcript | You can extract actionable insights from qualitative data |
| Usability testing | Task success rates + key failure points -> Design changes. Before/after for each finding | You validate designs with real users and iterate based on evidence |
| Survey data | Key findings as visualized data (charts). Statistical significance if you have it | You can gather and interpret quantitative data at scale |
| Competitive analysis | Feature comparison matrix with your positioning highlighted. NOT just screenshots of competitors | You understand the market context and make informed positioning decisions |
| Analytics-driven decisions | "Heatmap showed 70% of users never scrolled past the hero. Redesigned to surface key actions above fold" | You use data to identify problems, not just to validate your preferences |

## Tool Proficiency Without Resume-Listing

"Proficient in Figma, Sketch, Adobe XD, Illustrator, Photoshop" is meaningless. Demonstrate proficiency through the work.

| Instead of Listing | Demonstrate Through |
|---|---|
| "Expert in Figma" | Auto-layout usage visible in design. Component variants. Prototype with smart animate. Design tokens |
| "Proficient in user research" | Case study showing research methodology, sample sizes, and how findings influenced design |
| "Experience with design systems" | Actual design system you built or contributed to, with documentation |
| "Skilled in interaction design" | Prototype video showing micro-interactions, transitions, and state changes you designed |
| "Data-driven designer" | Case study where analytics/testing data drove design decisions, with before/after metrics |

## Portfolio-Specific Design Decisions

| Decision | For Design Portfolios | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Layout consistency | Use a consistent grid across case studies. Variations should be intentional, not accidental | Your portfolio IS a design project. Inconsistency in your portfolio signals inconsistency in your work |
| Typography | Max 2 typefaces. One for headings, one for body. Readable at body copy size on mobile | Hiring managers read case studies on their phones. If your portfolio is unreadable on mobile, that's a UX failure in your UX portfolio |
| Image quality | All screenshots at 2x resolution minimum. Consistent padding and device frames | Pixelated screenshots in a design portfolio is an instant credibility killer. Spend the time to export properly |
| White space | Generous. Design portfolios need breathing room. Text-heavy case studies feel like blog posts, not design work | More white space = higher perceived quality. The work should be the visual anchor, not walls of text |
| Color palette | Neutral portfolio palette that doesn't compete with the work. White/light gray background. One accent color for CTAs | A portfolio with an aggressive color palette fights your project screenshots for attention. Your work should pop, not your portfolio |
| Dark mode | Offer it if you can design it well. DON'T default to dark mode -- some work looks better on light backgrounds | Dark mode screenshots on a dark portfolio background = no contrast. Test your work screenshots in both modes |
