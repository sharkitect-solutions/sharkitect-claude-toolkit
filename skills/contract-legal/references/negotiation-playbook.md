# Contract Negotiation Playbook

Tactical framework for preparing, executing, and closing contract negotiations. Designed for SMB founders and operators who negotiate their own deals without in-house counsel.

## Pre-Negotiation Preparation

Complete this checklist before entering any negotiation. Unprepared negotiators make concessions they later regret.

### Information Gathering
- [ ] **Know your BATNA** (Best Alternative To Negotiated Agreement): What happens if this deal falls through? The stronger your alternative, the more leverage you have. If you have no alternative, you have no leverage -- fix that first.
- [ ] **Research counterparty's BATNA**: How many alternatives do they have? Are they under time pressure? Are they evaluating competitors? Their urgency determines your leverage.
- [ ] **Understand the relationship dynamic**: Are you the buyer or seller? Larger or smaller party? Repeat business or one-time? These dynamics shape which party has structural leverage on specific clauses.
- [ ] **Review counterparty's standard terms**: If they sent their template first, read every clause before your first meeting. Note every deviation from market standard.
- [ ] **Identify decision-makers**: Who on the counterparty's side has authority to approve terms? Negotiating with someone who needs to "check with legal" on every point doubles the timeline.
- [ ] **Set your walkaway point**: The specific terms below which you will not sign. Write this down before negotiating -- in the moment, anchoring bias and sunk cost will push you past your limits.

### Clause Priority Framework

Categorize every clause in the contract BEFORE entering negotiation:

**Must-Have (non-negotiable, walkaway triggers):**
- Liability cap at or below insurance coverage
- IP ownership of your pre-existing work and tools
- Payment terms that protect cash flow (Net-30 max, milestone-based)
- Termination for convenience with reasonable notice
- Mutual indemnification (not one-sided)

**Should-Have (important, negotiate hard but can flex on specifics):**
- Governing law in your home state
- Warranty period alignment with your delivery timeline
- Non-solicitation limited to 12 months
- Force majeure clause with termination right
- Dispute resolution with mediation step

**Nice-to-Have (preferred, use as trading chips):**
- Audit rights
- Quarterly business review obligations
- Reporting frequency preferences
- Specific notice delivery methods
- Right of first refusal on future work

**Concedable (willing to accept counterparty's version):**
- Governing law (if jurisdiction is reasonable and connected)
- Notice period details (email vs certified mail)
- Venue selection within reasonable geography
- Formatting and organizational preferences
- Non-material definitional preferences

### The Trade Matrix

Before your first negotiation session, build a trade matrix:

| Your Concedable | Their Likely Must-Have | Trade Value |
|-----------------|----------------------|-------------|
| Governing law flexibility | Their preferred jurisdiction | Medium -- costs you nothing if jurisdiction is fair |
| Expanded warranty period (60 to 90 days) | Longer protection window | Low -- quality work survives either period |
| Additional reporting | Visibility into project progress | Low -- you should be reporting anyway |
| Right of first refusal | Future engagement priority | Medium -- valuable relationship signal |

Use this matrix to make deliberate trades: "We can agree to your governing law preference if you agree to mutual indemnification." Never make unilateral concessions -- every give should have a get.

## Negotiation Execution

### Opening Moves

**Send your template first.** The party whose template forms the base controls the structure, definitions, and default positions. Every clause starts at your preferred position. The counterparty must redline specific objections rather than imposing their entire framework. Research consistently shows the first draft anchors the final outcome.

**If you receive their template first:** Do not edit their document. Create a comparison document showing their terms vs your standard terms for each clause. Present deviations as "areas to align" rather than rejections.

### Reading Redlines

When you receive a redlined contract:

1. **Categorize every change** using the Must-Have/Should-Have/Nice-to-Have/Concedable framework
2. **Count the changes**: More than 20 substantive redlines on a standard MSA suggests the counterparty is using negotiation as a control mechanism. Consider whether the relationship will be collaborative.
3. **Identify the pattern**: Are changes clustered around liability and risk? They are risk-averse. Around payment? They have cash flow concerns. Around IP? They have had bad experiences with ownership disputes.
4. **Respond strategically**: Accept Concedable changes immediately (builds goodwill). Flag Must-Have disagreements early (prevents late-stage surprises). Bundle Should-Have items for package negotiation.

### Common Counterparty Tactics and Responses

**The Anchor:** Counterparty opens with an extreme position (e.g., unlimited liability, 120-day payment terms) to shift the negotiation midpoint.
*Response:* Ignore the anchor. Present your own starting position as if theirs does not exist. "Our standard terms are [X], which aligns with industry practice."

**The Nibble:** After reaching agreement on major terms, counterparty adds small last-minute requests ("Can we also add a 90-day exclusivity clause?").
*Response:* Treat every nibble as a reopening. "Adding that term changes the balance of the agreement. If we add exclusivity, we would need to revisit the pricing to reflect that constraint."

**The Good Cop / Bad Cop:** Business contact is supportive ("I want to make this work") while their legal team rejects everything ("Legal won't approve that").
*Response:* Negotiate directly with the decision-maker. Request a joint call with both business and legal present. "Let's get everyone in the same room so we can resolve these points efficiently."

**The Deadline Squeeze:** "We need this signed by Friday or the budget gets reallocated."
*Response:* Artificial deadlines create bad contracts. "We want to move quickly too, but signing terms we have not reviewed creates risk for both sides. A one-week extension protects us both."

**The Precedent Claim:** "We have never agreed to that clause with any vendor."
*Response:* "That may be the case, but this clause reflects standard industry practice. If it has never come up, it may be because previous vendors did not protect their interests adequately. We are happy to discuss variations."

**The Volume Discount Ask:** "We will bring much more business if you agree to these terms on this first deal."
*Response:* "We would love to grow together. Let's structure this deal at standard terms, and we can negotiate volume-based adjustments in a follow-on agreement once the relationship is established."

## Escalation Strategies When Negotiations Stall

### Level 1: Reframe the Impasse (Day 1-3)
Shift from positions to interests. Instead of "We need a 12-month liability cap" vs "We need unlimited liability," ask: "What risk are you trying to protect against?" Often the underlying interest can be addressed through alternative mechanisms (additional insurance, escrow, performance bonds).

### Level 2: Package Deal (Day 3-7)
Bundle multiple contested clauses into a single package offer. "We will agree to your governing law, extend the warranty to 90 days, and add quarterly reporting if you agree to mutual indemnification, a 12-month liability cap, and Net-30 payment terms." Packages allow both sides to claim wins.

### Level 3: Bring in Principals (Day 7-14)
If working-level negotiators are stuck, escalate to decision-makers (CEO to CEO or VP to VP). Principals can make business trade-offs that legal teams cannot. Frame the escalation as seeking a business solution, not overriding legal advice.

### Level 4: Propose a Compromise Mechanism (Day 14-21)
For truly contested terms, propose time-limited provisions. "Let's cap liability at 6 months of fees for the first year, then revisit based on the actual risk profile of the engagement." Or propose an escrow mechanism for disputed financial terms.

### Level 5: Walk Away (Day 21+)
If Must-Have terms remain unresolved after good-faith negotiation, walk away. State your position clearly: "We have been unable to reach agreement on [specific terms]. These are non-negotiable for us because [specific business reason]. We would welcome the opportunity to revisit this if your position changes." Walking away preserves the relationship for future opportunities. Signing bad terms poisons it.

## When to Walk Away -- Quantified Criteria

Walk away from any deal where:

- **Liability exposure exceeds 3x the contract value** -- The risk-reward ratio is inverted. A $50K contract with unlimited liability is not a $50K deal; it is an uncapped risk position.
- **Payment terms create cash flow distress** -- If you must fund more than 60 days of project costs before receiving payment, the contract is a loan to the client at 0% interest.
- **IP assignment captures pre-existing work** -- If the contract requires assigning IP you developed before this engagement, you are selling your business infrastructure, not your services.
- **Indemnification is uncapped and one-sided** -- You bear unlimited financial risk for categories of harm you cannot control. One lawsuit could exceed your total business value.
- **Termination provisions are asymmetric** -- The counterparty can exit at will while you are locked in. This is a lease, not a partnership.
- **The negotiation process itself is adversarial** -- If the counterparty negotiates in bad faith (retracting agreed terms, introducing new demands at signing, misrepresenting their authority), the contract relationship will be worse.

## Post-Signature Obligations Tracking

Signing the contract is not the finish line. Track these ongoing obligations:

| Obligation | Tracking Method | Failure Consequence |
|-----------|----------------|-------------------|
| Payment deadlines | Calendar alerts 7 days before due | Late fees, service suspension rights |
| Insurance maintenance | Annual renewal reminders, certificate delivery | Breach of representations, indemnification risk |
| Confidentiality compliance | Annual staff training, access audits | Breach liability, injunctive relief exposure |
| SLA reporting | Automated monitoring and monthly reports | Service credit triggers, termination for cause |
| Renewal/termination windows | Calendar alerts 90 days before dates | Auto-renewal into unfavorable terms |
| Deliverable deadlines | Project management tool linked to SOW milestones | Breach of contract, termination for cause |
| Non-solicitation expiration | Calendar alert at end of restricted period | Continued unnecessary restriction on hiring |

**Post-signature rule:** Create a "Contract Obligations" tracker for every signed agreement. Review quarterly. The most common breach is not intentional violation -- it is forgetting an obligation exists.
