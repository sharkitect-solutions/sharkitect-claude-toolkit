# Industry Tone Calibration Guide

Load when editing copy for a specific industry where regulatory language, audience expectations, or conversion patterns differ from generic marketing copy.

## Industry-Specific Editing Rules

### SaaS / B2B Software

| Editing Rule | Why | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Keep technical terms the audience uses daily | SaaS buyers are practitioners -- simplifying "CI/CD pipeline" to "deployment process" feels condescending | "Automate your CI/CD pipeline" not "Automate your software deployment process" |
| Replace vanity metrics with workflow metrics | SaaS buyers care about time saved, not impressions | "Cut PR review time from 4 hours to 20 minutes" not "Boost team productivity" |
| Remove "seamless integration" -- always | Every SaaS product claims seamless integration; it's invisible to buyers | Specify: "Connects to Slack, Jira, and GitHub in under 5 minutes" |
| Preserve technical precision over emotional appeal | B2B buyers make rational, committee-approved purchases | "99.9% uptime SLA with automated failover" not "Never worry about downtime again" |
| Watch for feature-benefit confusion | SaaS copy lists features as benefits constantly | "Automated backups every 6 hours" is a feature; "Recover any file from the last 30 days with one click" is the benefit |

**SaaS voice spectrum**: Developer tools = direct, terse, no exclamation marks. Enterprise = authoritative, third-person acceptable. SMB = conversational, second-person, empathetic.

### E-Commerce / DTC

| Editing Rule | Why | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Sensory language over adjective stacking | Shoppers need to imagine touching/using the product | "Butter-soft leather that molds to your grip" not "Premium high-quality genuine leather" |
| Urgency must be real or absent | Fake urgency ("Only 3 left!") erodes trust; real urgency converts | "Pre-order closes March 15 -- production run of 500 units" is real. "Limited time offer!" with no end date is fake. |
| Size/fit/material specifics over superlatives | Returns cost 15-30% of item value; specific details prevent returns | "Runs one size small -- order up. 97% cotton, 3% elastane, 220gsm weight." |
| Social proof must include context | "5 stars" means nothing without context | "4.8 stars from 2,847 reviews -- rated #1 for durability by Wirecutter 2025" |
| Shorten everything near the buy button | Decision fatigue at checkout kills conversion | Within 2 scrolls of "Add to Cart": price, key differentiator, trust signal. Nothing else. |

**DTC voice trap**: Overly casual brands ("Hey bestie!") alienate 35+ demographics. Match casualness to actual customer age distribution, not brand aspiration.

### Financial Services

| Editing Rule | Why | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Never imply guaranteed returns | SEC/FCA/ASIC regulations prohibit performance promises | "Historically returned 7-9% annually" with required disclaimer, never "Earn 8% on your money" |
| Compliance language is non-negotiable -- edit around it | Legal disclaimers can't be rewritten for brevity | Edit the marketing copy to flow naturally INTO the disclaimer, not despite it |
| Numbers need attribution and timeframes | Financial claims without sources are regulatory violations | "S&P 500 returned 10.7% annually from 1957-2023 (source: NYU Stern)" |
| Replace emotional urgency with rational urgency | Finance audiences distrust hype | "Tax-loss harvesting deadline is December 31" not "Don't miss out on huge tax savings!" |
| "Simple" must mean actually simple | Calling a complex product "simple" when it has 12 fee categories is misleading | Describe the actual process: "Open an account in 8 minutes. Three questions, one ID upload, done." |

**Regulatory red words**: "guarantee," "promise," "safe," "risk-free," "no risk," "assured returns" -- flag every instance for legal review. Even in testimonials.

### Healthcare / Wellness

| Editing Rule | Why | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Claims require clinical evidence or must use hedging language | FTC enforces health claims aggressively; FDA monitors supplement marketing | "Studies suggest..." or "May help support..." never "Cures," "Treats," or "Prevents" |
| Patient/user stories need disclaimers | Individual results aren't generalizable | Add: "Individual results may vary. Not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease." |
| Empathy before solution | Healthcare audiences are often in pain or distress | Acknowledge the problem first: "Living with chronic back pain changes everything." Then offer the solution. |
| Remove before/after implications unless clinically documented | FTC cracks down on transformation marketing in health | "Clinical trial participants reported 40% reduction in symptoms (n=200, double-blind)" is defensible. Anecdotal before/after is not. |
| Accessibility language matters | Healthcare audiences include disabled, elderly, and non-native speakers | Grade 6-8 reading level. Short sentences. Define medical terms on first use. |

### Agency / Professional Services

| Editing Rule | Why | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Remove "we" stacking -- more than 3 per paragraph | Services copy over-indexes on "we do this, we do that" | Reframe around client outcomes: "Your campaigns launch 40% faster" not "We streamline your campaign process" |
| Case studies need specifics, not praise | "We helped Company X grow" is meaningless | "Increased Company X's qualified leads from 120/mo to 890/mo in 6 months (paid + organic)" |
| Don't oversell the process | Clients buy outcomes, not methodologies | One sentence on process, then the result: "Through competitive analysis and repositioning, we moved Brand Y from #4 to #1 in category awareness (Kantar, Q3 2024)" |
| Credential proof > self-description | "Award-winning" without specifying which award is empty | "Webby Award 2024, Best B2B Website" not "Award-winning agency" |

## Cross-Industry Editing Traps

| Trap | Industries Affected | How to Catch |
|---|---|---|
| Jargon calibration mismatch | All | Read the copy imagining you're the least technical person on the buying committee. If they'd be lost, simplify. If they'd be insulted by simplification, keep the jargon. |
| Regulatory language removal | Finance, Healthcare, Legal | Never edit or compress regulatory disclaimers. Edit the marketing copy around them instead. Flag any changes to compliance text for legal review. |
| Aspiration-reality gap | DTC, Wellness, SaaS | If the "after" state described in the copy requires 6 months of effort but reads like an instant transformation, close the gap with honest timelines. |
| Competitor comparison without substantiation | SaaS, E-commerce | "Faster than Competitor X" needs a benchmark. Unsubstantiated comparison claims invite legal action AND erode trust. |
| Template voice bleeding | Agencies serving multiple clients | Check that edits match THIS client's voice, not the last client you edited for. Reset by re-reading 3-5 published pieces from the brand before each editing session. |

## Tone Spectrum Reference

| Dimension | Formal End | Casual End | Decision Factor |
|---|---|---|---|
| Contractions | "We will not" | "We won't" | B2B enterprise = few contractions. DTC/startup = natural contractions. |
| Sentence length | 20-25 words average | 8-15 words average | Complex products need longer explanation sentences. Simple products need punchy copy. |
| Exclamation marks | Zero | 1-2 per page maximum | Finance/legal/enterprise = never. DTC/lifestyle = sparingly. More than 2 per page = amateur. |
| Emoji in copy | Never | Sparingly in headlines/CTAs | Only if brand uses them in existing published content. Never introduce emoji during editing. |
| First person | "The company" or "Our team" | "I" or "We" | Solo founder = "I". Team of 10+ = "We". Corporation = "Our team at [Brand]". |
| Humor | None | Light, relevant wit | Only if existing brand voice includes humor. Never add humor during editing -- it's a brand decision, not an editing decision. |
