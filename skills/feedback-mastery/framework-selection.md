# Feedback Framework Selection Guide

## Framework Overview

Multiple feedback frameworks exist. Each has strengths, limitations, and situations where it fails. Choosing the right one matters more than executing any one perfectly.

### Framework Comparison

| Framework | Structure | Best For | Fails When |
|---|---|---|---|
| **SBI** (Situation-Behavior-Impact) | Specific situation -> Observable behavior -> Measurable impact | Simple behavioral feedback, performance conversations, documentation | Impact is emotional (not business), cultural context requires indirectness, recipient needs feelings acknowledged first |
| **COIN** (Context-Observation-Impact-Next steps) | Organizational context -> Observation -> Impact -> Proposed next steps | Linking individual behavior to organizational goals, when you want forward-looking action | Recipient feels lectured ("here's what you should do"). Works better top-down than peer-to-peer |
| **DESC** (Describe-Express-Specify-Consequences) | Describe behavior -> Express feelings -> Specify desired change -> Consequences | Situations where YOUR feelings are the impact, peer relationships, when emotional honesty matters | Formal settings where feelings feel inappropriate, when the recipient dismisses emotional impact |
| **CEDAR** (Context-Examples-Diagnosis-Action-Review) | Context -> Examples -> Collaborative diagnosis -> Agreed action -> Review plan | Performance improvement plans, pattern behavior requiring deep exploration | Quick feedback moments, minor issues, when you need to be direct and brief |
| **Radical Candor** (Care Personally + Challenge Directly) | Show you care -> Be direct about the issue | High-trust relationships, Stage 4 psychological safety, cultures that value directness | Low psychological safety (perceived as aggression), high power distance cultures, new relationships |
| **Nonviolent Communication** (Observation-Feeling-Need-Request) | Observation -> Your feeling -> Underlying need -> Specific request | Conflict resolution, emotionally charged situations, relationship repair | Business contexts where "I feel" language seems unprofessional (perception varies by culture) |

## Framework Selection Decision

First-match: use the FIRST row where your situation matches.

| Your Situation | Framework | Opening Line |
|---|---|---|
| Safety/ethics violation, needs immediate correction | **Direct SBI** -- no softening | "I need to talk about something serious. [Situation]. [Behavior]. [Impact]. This needs to change immediately." |
| One specific recent incident, clear business impact | **SBI** | "In [situation], when you [behavior], it [impact]. Can you help me understand what happened?" |
| Pattern behavior (3+ occurrences), needs improvement plan | **CEDAR** | "I want to discuss a pattern I've noticed. Can we explore this together and find a way forward?" |
| You need to express how their behavior makes you FEEL | **DESC** | "When [behavior], I feel [emotion] because [reason]. I'd like [specific change]." |
| Need to connect behavior to team/company goals | **COIN** | "In the context of [goal], I observed [behavior], which impacted [outcome]. Here's what I think would help..." |
| Emotional conflict, relationship damage, need repair | **NVC** | "When I see [observation], I feel [feeling] because I need [need]. Would you be willing to [request]?" |
| High-trust relationship, can be fully direct | **Radical Candor** | "I care about your growth, so I want to be direct: [issue]. Here's what I'm seeing and why it matters." |

## SBI Deep Dive: When It Works and When It Doesn't

### SBI Strengths

| Strength | Why It Matters | Research |
|---|---|---|
| **Observable facts reduce defensiveness** | Brain processes factual statements differently from judgments. Facts activate prefrontal cortex (analytical). Judgments activate amygdala (defensive) | Lieberman et al. (2007): affect labeling reduces amygdala response by 43% |
| **Specificity enables action** | Vague feedback ("be more proactive") gives no actionable path. Specific feedback ("in yesterday's meeting, you waited for others to volunteer") provides a concrete moment to reflect on | Kluger & DeNisi (1996): specific feedback improves performance in 60% of cases vs 38% for general feedback |
| **Impact links behavior to consequences** | Without impact, behavior seems arbitrary. "Don't interrupt" is a rule. "Interrupting prevented the team from hearing the full analysis" is a reason | London (2003): feedback with clear rationale is 2.5x more likely to change behavior |

### SBI Limitations

| Limitation | What Happens | Alternative |
|---|---|---|
| **Impact must be business-relevant** | If the impact is "it annoyed me," SBI feels petty. Not every behavior has a clear business impact | Use DESC for emotion-primary feedback. "When you [behavior], I feel [feeling] because [need]" |
| **Observation without interpretation is sometimes impossible** | "You seemed disengaged" is an interpretation. But "you looked at your phone" may not capture the real issue (lack of participation, not phone use) | Name the interpretation explicitly: "I may be wrong, but my impression was [interpretation]. Can you help me understand?" |
| **Cultural mismatch** | Direct behavioral description is considered rude in high-context cultures (Japan, Korea, many Middle Eastern cultures). The "directness" that makes SBI effective in Western cultures creates shame in others | Use Indirect SBI: soften with questions. "I noticed [situation]. What was your thinking about [behavior]?" Let them self-identify the impact |
| **Power amplification** | When a manager uses SBI, the structure's precision can feel like a formal reprimand even for minor feedback | Add warmth before structure: "This is minor, but I want to mention it because I think it'll help you. [SBI]" |

## Cultural Adaptation Matrix

Feedback norms vary dramatically across cultures. Using the wrong approach creates more damage than giving no feedback at all.

| Cultural Dimension (Hofstede) | Low Score (examples) | High Score (examples) | Feedback Adaptation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Power Distance** | Scandinavian countries, Netherlands, Australia | Japan, Korea, India, many LATAM countries | High PD: never give public feedback to someone senior. Frame as question: "Would it be helpful if I shared an observation?" Low PD: direct feedback is expected and respected |
| **Individualism vs Collectivism** | US, UK, Australia, Netherlands | Japan, China, Korea, most of Asia | Collectivist: frame feedback in terms of team harmony, not individual performance. "The team would benefit from..." not "You should..." |
| **Uncertainty Avoidance** | Singapore, Denmark, UK | Japan, Greece, Portugal, Belgium | High UA: provide clear, specific steps for improvement. Ambiguous feedback ("think about it") causes anxiety. Low UA: open-ended exploration works |
| **Direct vs Indirect Communication** | Germany, Netherlands, Israel, US | Japan, UK (indirect despite being Western), most of Asia | Indirect cultures: use stories, analogies, or third-party examples. "I've seen situations where..." rather than "You did..." |

### Practical Rules for Cross-Cultural Feedback

| Rule | Example |
|---|---|
| **Ask how they prefer to receive feedback** | "Different people prefer different approaches. Would you rather I be very direct, or would you prefer I share observations and we discuss?" |
| **Start with questions, not statements** | "How do you think the presentation went?" before "Here's what I noticed about the presentation" |
| **Use "we" language in collectivist contexts** | "How can WE improve this process?" not "How can YOU do better?" |
| **Provide written follow-up in high-UA cultures** | The conversation is the opening. The written summary with specific steps is what they'll actually act on |
| **Adjust formality to match their expectation** | Casual feedback to someone who expects formality feels dismissive. Formal feedback to someone who expects casualness feels threatening |

## Common Framework Mistakes

| Mistake | What People Do | Why It Fails | Fix |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Framework worship** | Memorize SBI and use it for EVERY feedback situation regardless of context | Frameworks are tools, not scripts. Rigid application feels robotic and misses nuance | Choose framework based on situation (see decision table above). Adapt language to your natural style |
| **Feedback without relationship** | Use a "good framework" but have no relationship with the recipient | Framework structure doesn't create trust. Trust creates receptivity. Without trust, any framework feels like an attack | Build relationship first. Minimum 3 positive interactions before first corrective feedback. No shortcut |
| **Skipping the other side** | Deliver perfect SBI statement, then wait for compliance | Feedback is dialogue. The recipient's perspective may change your understanding of the situation entirely | After your SBI, ask: "What's your take?" and genuinely listen. Be prepared to learn you were wrong |
| **Overcomplicating simple feedback** | Using CEDAR for "hey, your mic was muted" | Not every observation needs a framework. Frameworks are for feedback with emotional weight or pattern significance | Quick corrections don't need frameworks. "Hey, heads up, your mic's muted" is fine. Save frameworks for things that matter |
| **Using the framework as a shield** | "I'm using SBI, so you can't be upset" | The framework doesn't prevent emotional reactions. It reduces defensiveness, not eliminates it | Expect emotional reactions. Have a plan for them (see conversation-dynamics.md). The framework helps YOU prepare, not protect you from their response |
